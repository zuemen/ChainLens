## Commercial blockchain analytics systems (Chainalysis Reactor/KYT, Elliptic Navigator/Lens/Investigator, TRM Labs)

### basic_info

- **name**: Commercial blockchain analytics systems (Chainalysis Reactor/KYT, Elliptic Navigator/Lens/Investigator, TRM Labs)
- **item_type**: system
- **year**: 2013-2026 (Chainalysis founded 2014, Elliptic 2013, TRM Labs 2018; capabilities described reflect 2024-2026 product state)
- **authors_or_org**: Chainalysis Inc. (Reactor, KYT, Address Screening, VASP Risking, Community); Elliptic (Navigator, Lens, Investigator, Discovery); TRM Labs (Blockchain Intelligence, Forensics, Orion/Co-Case AI agents)

### method

- **core_method**: Commercial-grade blockchain analytics suites that turn raw on-chain transaction data into compliance-actionable intelligence. Three functional layers dominate all three vendors: (1) ATTRIBUTION + ENTITY CLUSTERING - proprietary databases mapping addresses to real-world entities (exchanges, mixers, darknet markets, ransomware, sanctioned actors) built from co-spend/common-input-ownership heuristics, change-address detection, exchange deposit-address identification, direct exchange cooperation (KYC-linked ground truth), darknet monitoring, OSINT and undercover work. Chainalysis emphasizes 'ground-truth' attributions; TRM stresses 150+ risk indicators and multi-input/graph-traversal clustering; Elliptic emphasizes breadth (60+ chains, 250+ bridges). (2) RISK SCORING (KYT/KYA) - every address/transaction/wallet receives a dynamic risk score computed from DIRECT and INDIRECT (multi-hop) counterparty exposure to risk categories (sanctions, DNM, ransomware, mixers, scams), weighted by transaction directionality, amount and configurable time windows; scores update as models ingest new data; thresholds/category severities are customer-tunable to risk appetite. (3) INVESTIGATION UX - a visual transaction-graph explorer (Chainalysis Reactor, Elliptic Investigator, TRM Forensics) letting analysts expand transaction paths, follow funds cross-chain/cross-asset through bridges/DEXs/swaps/mixers, apply date/amount filters, annotate nodes, use timeline views, and export court-ready PDF evidence packages (graph + attributions + risk scores + methodology). Increasingly augmented with LLM/AI copilots (TRM Orion & Co-Case Agent; Chainalysis is adding AI investigative assistants; Elliptic natural-language querying).
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Yes. Directionality is first-class: exposure scoring distinguishes incoming vs outgoing flows, edge attributes (amount, asset, timestamp) are exploited, and cross-chain/bridge/DEX/swap edges are stitched into a unified directed multigraph for tracing (Chainalysis: 27+ chains, 325M+ swaps, 300+ bridges/DEXs; Elliptic: 60+ chains, 250+ bridges).
- **typology_coverage**: Broad and productized as 'typology libraries' / behavioral labels: sanctions exposure, darknet markets, ransomware, mixers/tumblers, fraud shops, terrorist financing, child-abuse material, scams (investment fraud, pig-butchering/romance scams, impersonation, giveaway scams), stolen funds/hacks, high-risk exchanges, gambling, and laundering structuring (smurfing/peel chains, layering across VASPs). TRM 'Transfer Labels' and Chainalysis category taxonomies encode these; typologies are continuously updated by in-house intelligence teams.
- **chain_coverage**: Multi-chain and cross-chain. Chainalysis: 27+ blockchains, 40M+ assets, 300+ bridges/DEXs, major mixers. Elliptic: 60+ blockchains, 250+ bridges, Bitcoin/Ethereum/stablecoins/ERC-20. TRM: broad multi-chain incl. Bitcoin, Ethereum, TRON, and many L1s/L2s with cross-chain tracing. All emphasize stablecoin and TRON/USDT coverage relevant to laundering.

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Investigation-centric, evidence-narrative explainability rather than ML attribution. Explanations take the form of the traced transaction graph, entity attributions, category exposure breakdowns (which risk categories, direct vs indirect, how many hops, what amounts), timeline reconstruction, analyst annotations, and auto-generated court-ready PDF evidence reports with a methodology section. AI copilots (TRM Orion/Co-Case, emerging Chainalysis/Elliptic assistants) add natural-language narrative summaries of findings.
- **explainability_type**: rule-/typology-based + evidence-narrative + exposure attribution (which flagged counterparties and categories drive the score); investigation-graph provenance. Not GNNExplainer/SHAP-style model attribution (models are proprietary and largely undisclosed).

### practicality

- **code_availability**: Closed-source, commercial SaaS + APIs. No open source. Integration is via vendor REST APIs/SDKs (e.g. Chainalysis KYT & Address Screening APIs, Elliptic screening APIs, TRM APIs). Licensed per-seat / per-volume.
- **data_availability**: Not publicly downloadable. Attribution/intelligence data is proprietary and access-controlled behind paid licenses and compliance agreements; no open dataset or license for the underlying labels.
- **scalability**: Production-grade, real-time at VASP/bank scale. Elliptic Navigator processes tens of thousands of transactions in real time; continuous monitoring and dynamic re-scoring run across 27-60+ chains and hundreds of millions of on-chain events. Built as horizontally-scaled cloud SaaS; TRM notably built high-throughput blockchain data infrastructure (BigQuery/large-scale pipelines). Real-time screening (KYT) and batch investigation both supported.
- **llm_integration**: annotation / end-to-end AI copilots (emerging). TRM Labs ships Orion and Co-Case Agent AI investigators; Chainalysis and Elliptic are adding AI investigative assistants and natural-language querying/narrative generation. LLMs are used for investigation acceleration, entity summarization and report drafting, not (publicly) as the core risk model.
- **deployment_maturity**: Production system (market-leading commercial products used by exchanges, banks, and law enforcement worldwide; the de-facto compliance stack VASPs are benchmarked against).
- **integration_difficulty**: For a VASP consumer: low-to-medium (call vendor KYT/screening APIs). For ChainLens as a BUILDER trying to replicate: high - the moat is the proprietary attribution/ground-truth database and intelligence operations, not the algorithms. ChainLens can realistically match investigation UX, typology motifs and risk-exposure scoring on open data (Elliptic/TRON) but cannot replicate the labeled entity graph without equivalent data-sourcing.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: This is the product benchmark ChainLens is implicitly competing with and should emulate at the UX/capability level even if not at the data-moat level. The three vendors define what a VASP compliance tool is EXPECTED to have: (a) entity clustering/attribution, (b) direct+indirect multi-hop risk scoring with tunable thresholds, (c) a typology library (smurfing, peel chains, mixers, pig-butchering, sanctions/DNM/ransomware), (d) a visual cross-chain investigation graph with fund tracing, filtering, annotation and court-ready evidence export, and (e) continuous monitoring/alerting. ChainLens (SNA features + GCN/GraphSAGE + rule motifs + evidence narrative on Elliptic + TRON TRC-20) already aligns architecturally: its rule motifs = their typology library, its evidence narrative = their PDF evidence report, its GNN risk output = their risk score. The clearest differentiation path for ChainLens is transparent/open ML explainability (GNNExplainer/attention/motif attribution) versus their opaque proprietary scores, plus focus on TRON/USDT laundering and low-cost/CPU feasibility. ChainLens should adopt their exposure model (score the CLUSTER via direct + N-hop counterparty exposure to labeled bad categories, weighted by amount/time/direction) and their investigation-graph + evidence-export UX as table-stakes.
- **key_takeaways**: Table-stakes for a VASP compliance tool = five capabilities: entity clustering/attribution, direct+indirect multi-hop risk scoring (tunable thresholds), a maintained typology library, a visual cross-chain investigation graph with fund tracing + annotation + court-ready evidence export, and continuous monitoring/alerting. ChainLens should map its features 1:1 onto these.<br>The real moat is the proprietary ground-truth attribution database (exchange cooperation, subpoenas, OSINT, undercover ops), not the algorithms - ChainLens cannot out-label them, so it should compete on transparent/open explainability, TRON/USDT specialization, and cost.<br>Adopt the exposure-based risk model: score the entity/cluster (not just an address) by direct and indirect counterparty exposure to labeled risk categories, weighted by amount, direction and a configurable time window; re-score continuously.<br>Investigation UX is a product, not an afterthought: timeline view, path expansion, filters, cross-chain/bridge stitching, node annotation, and one-click PDF evidence packages with a methodology section are expected - ChainLens's evidence-narrative layer should target this exact output.<br>AI copilots (TRM Orion/Co-Case, emerging Chainalysis/Elliptic assistants) are the current frontier for natural-language investigation and report generation - a strong differentiation lane where ChainLens's LLM evidence-narrative can lead with transparency rather than replicate an opaque score.

## Elliptic Dataset and Weber et al. 2019 (Anti-Money Laundering in Bitcoin: Experimenting with Graph Convolutional Networks for Financial Forensics)

### basic_info

- **name**: Elliptic Dataset and Weber et al. 2019 (Anti-Money Laundering in Bitcoin: Experimenting with Graph Convolutional Networks for Financial Forensics)
- **item_type**: dataset
- **year**: 2019
- **authors_or_org**: Mark Weber, Giacomo Domeniconi, Jie Chen, Daniel Karl I. Weidele, Claudio Bellei, Tom Robinson, Charles E. Leiserson. Joint work of the MIT-IBM Watson AI Lab and Elliptic. Presented at the KDD 2019 Anomaly Detection in Finance Workshop (arXiv:1908.02591).

### method

- **core_method**: Introduces the Elliptic dataset and benchmarks a set of node-classification models to flag illicit Bitcoin transactions. Each transaction is a node with 166 features: 94 'local' features (LF) derived directly from the transaction (time step, inputs/outputs, fees, volume, aggregated BTC amounts, average neighbors) plus 72 'aggregated' features (AF) formed by one-hop aggregation (max, min, std, correlations) of a node's neighbor local features. Baselines: (1) Logistic Regression and (2) Random Forest (RandomForest with 50 estimators, scikit-learn) as non-graph classifiers on LF and LF+AF; (3) Multilayer Perceptron (MLP); (4) a 2-layer Graph Convolutional Network (GCN, Kipf & Welling); (5) Skip-GCN, a GCN variant that concatenates a skip connection from the raw node features to the last GCN layer so the model retains access to the node's own features alongside neighborhood aggregation; and (6) EvolveGCN as a temporal extension. Training uses a weighted cross-entropy loss to counter class imbalance. The headline empirical result is that Random Forest with all features (F1=0.788) beats the vanilla GCN (F1=0.628), while Skip-GCN (F1=0.705) and EvolveGCN (F1=0.720) close much of the gap.
- **graph_task_type**: node (transaction-level binary node classification: illicit vs licit)
- **temporal_modeling**: The graph is a directed time-series graph split into 49 discrete time steps (each ~an evenly-spaced sampling of ~2 weeks of blocks; within a time step the sub-graph of transactions appearing within ~3 hours is connected, and time steps are disjoint). The canonical evaluation is a strict temporal split: time steps 1-34 for training and 35-49 for test (roughly 70/30), so the model must generalize forward in time. The paper's EvolveGCN experiment explicitly models temporal dynamics by evolving GCN weights across time steps with an RNN. A key observation: a dark-market shutdown around time step 43 causes a sharp, sustained drop in illicit recall for all models, illustrating that AML models degrade when the statistical regime shifts (emerging/novel behaviors).
- **class_imbalance_handling**: Extreme imbalance: of 203,769 transactions only ~2% (4,545) are labeled illicit, ~21% (42,019) licit, and ~77% (157,205) unlabeled/unknown. The unlabeled nodes are kept in the graph (for message passing / structure) but excluded from the supervised loss. Imbalance is handled via a class-weighted cross-entropy loss (weight 0.3/0.7 toward the illicit minority class for the neural models). Evaluation deliberately focuses on illicit-class F1 (not accuracy) because of the imbalance.
- **heterophily_handling**: Not explicitly addressed. The paper notes GCN's neighborhood averaging (homophily assumption) as a likely reason vanilla GCN underperforms Random Forest, and proposes Skip-GCN (skip connection preserving a node's own features) as a partial mitigation so a node is not entirely smoothed toward its neighbors. Camouflage / fraudster-to-normal linking is discussed qualitatively but no dedicated heterophily module is used.
- **supervision_regime**: Supervised (binary) on the labeled subset, with the large unlabeled set retained for graph structure; effectively a semi-supervised transductive setup for the GCN variants.
- **edge_and_direction_support**: The graph is a directed payment-flow multigraph (234,355 directed edges) but the released node features are largely precomputed aggregates; the GCN baselines consume the graph adjacency (used symmetrically for message passing) rather than explicit per-edge features. Directionality and edge attributes (amount, time) are encoded only implicitly through the aggregated node features, not as first-class edge features in the models.
- **typology_coverage**: No explicit laundering-typology taxonomy (no per-pattern labels for smurfing, gather-scatter, peeling, mixers). Illicit labels come from Elliptic's proprietary heuristics/real-world entity attribution (scams, malware, terrorist orgs, Ponzi schemes, darknet markets). Typologies are therefore covered only implicitly through whatever the illicit label captures.
- **chain_coverage**: Bitcoin (single chain).

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: The Elliptic dataset (contributed by this paper): 203,769 transaction nodes, 234,355 directed edges, 166 features, 49 time steps. At publication it was the largest labeled transaction dataset of any cryptocurrency. Publicly released on Kaggle and now bundled in PyTorch Geometric (EllipticBitcoinDataset).
- **key_metrics**: Canonical illicit-class results on the official temporal split (train 1-34 / test 35-49), Precision / Recall / F1 (and Micro-AVG F1): Logistic Regression LF 0.348 / 0.668 / 0.457 (0.920); Logistic Regression AF 0.404 / 0.593 / 0.481 (0.931); Random Forest LF 0.803 / 0.611 / 0.694 (0.966); Random Forest AF (all features) 0.956 / 0.670 / 0.788 (0.977) -- the strongest and the canonical illicit-F1 number to beat; MLP LF 0.637 / 0.662 / 0.649 (0.958); MLP AF 0.694 / 0.617 / 0.653 (0.962); GCN 0.812 / 0.512 / 0.628 (0.961); Skip-GCN 0.812 / 0.623 / 0.705 (0.966); EvolveGCN (temporal) 0.850 / 0.624 / 0.720 (0.968). Takeaway: tree ensemble Random Forest on hand-crafted + 1-hop-aggregated features (illicit F1 = 0.788) outperforms vanilla GCN; graph/temporal methods narrow but do not close the gap. Recall for every model collapses after the ~time-step-43 dark-market shutdown.

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Minimal in the original paper. Provides qualitative interpretability via 2D t-SNE / GCN embedding visualizations of licit vs illicit clusters and discussion of feature importance from the Random Forest, but no formal attribution or subgraph-explanation method. The team's companion vision (later work) proposes interactive visual analytics for investigators.
- **explainability_type**: SHAP-LIME / rule-based (weak): Random Forest feature importances plus embedding visualizations; no dedicated GNN explainer.

### practicality

- **code_availability**: Open source. Reference implementations and baselines are widely reproduced; PyTorch Geometric ships EllipticBitcoinDataset and example scripts; the EvolveGCN code (IBM) is on GitHub (github.com/IBM/EvolveGCN). No single official repo from the KDD workshop paper, but numerous faithful reimplementations exist.
- **data_availability**: Publicly downloadable. Released on Kaggle (ellipticco/elliptic-data-set) and via PyTorch Geometric. Features are anonymized (feature meanings not fully disclosed; only ~the first two of the 166 are named as time step and transaction id). License is research/non-commercial as posted by Elliptic.
- **scalability**: Modest scale by modern GNN standards (~204k nodes, ~234k edges, 166 features) -- trivially fits in memory and trains on CPU or a single GPU in minutes. Random Forest and Logistic Regression run comfortably on CPU. Real-time scoring of individual transactions is feasible once features are computed; the bottleneck in production would be computing the 1-hop aggregated features on a live, much larger chain.
- **llm_integration**: none
- **deployment_maturity**: academic prototype / benchmark (the dataset itself became the de-facto community benchmark; Elliptic the company runs production AML but this specific release is a research benchmark).
- **integration_difficulty**: low: the dataset is small, standardized, and directly loadable via PyTorch Geometric; reproducing the RF/GCN/Skip-GCN baselines is a well-trodden path. The main friction for ChainLens is that Elliptic features are anonymized precomputed aggregates, so you cannot re-derive raw SNA/graph features from them or map them onto TRON TRC-20 data without building an equivalent feature pipeline.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: This is the foundational benchmark ChainLens must anchor against. ChainLens's planned stack (social-network-analysis features + GCN/GraphSAGE + rule-based motifs + evidence narrative) is essentially a superset of Weber et al.'s design, so the paper defines both the baseline to beat and the exact evaluation protocol to adopt: use the official temporal split (train 1-34, test 35-49) and report illicit-class F1 / precision / recall, targeting the canonical Random Forest-AF illicit F1 = 0.788 and Skip-GCN 0.705 as the numbers to exceed. The key lesson is that a Random Forest on well-engineered local + 1-hop aggregated features beats a naive GCN, so ChainLens should (a) invest in strong hand-crafted SNA/aggregate features as a Random Forest baseline, (b) use Skip-GCN-style skip connections (or GraphSAGE, which already concatenates self features) so message passing does not smooth away a node's own signal, and (c) always evaluate temporally, watching for the time-step-43-style regime shift that tanks recall -- a direct argument for ChainLens's rule/motif layer and evidence narrative to catch novel patterns that a purely learned model misses. The Elliptic dataset is also the natural first training/validation set before extending to TRON TRC-20.
- **key_takeaways**: Adopt the exact canonical protocol: Elliptic official temporal split (train time steps 1-34, test 35-49), report illicit-class F1; the numbers to beat are Random Forest (all features) F1=0.788, Skip-GCN F1=0.705, EvolveGCN F1=0.720, vanilla GCN F1=0.628.<br>A strong Random Forest on local + 1-hop aggregated features beats vanilla GCN -- ChainLens should keep a tuned tree-ensemble baseline and heavy feature engineering, not assume a GNN wins by default.<br>Skip connections matter: Skip-GCN >> GCN because a node keeps access to its own features; prefer GraphSAGE / skip-GCN over vanilla GCN to combat over-smoothing and camouflage.<br>Temporal evaluation is mandatory: the dark-market shutdown at ~time step 43 collapses recall for every model, proving learned AML models degrade under regime shift -- justifies ChainLens's rule/motif + narrative layer for novel behavior.<br>Extreme 2% illicit imbalance means optimize and report on illicit F1/PR-AUC (never accuracy) and use class-weighted loss; keep unlabeled nodes in the graph for structure but out of the loss.

## Elliptic++ (2023) and Elliptic2 (2024) datasets

### basic_info

- **name**: Elliptic++ (2023) and Elliptic2 (2024) datasets
- **item_type**: dataset
- **year**: 2024
- **authors_or_org**: Elliptic++: Youssef Elmougy and Ling Liu (Georgia Institute of Technology), 'Demystifying Fraudulent Transactions and Illicit Nodes in the Bitcoin Network for Financial Forensics', KDD 2023 (arXiv:2306.06108). Elliptic2: Claudio Bellei, Muhua Xu, Ross Phillips, Tom Robinson, Mark Weber, Tim Kaler, Charles E. Leiserson, Arvind (John) Chen, Jie Chen -- collaboration of Elliptic, MIT CSAIL, MIT-IBM Watson AI Lab, MIT Media Lab, and IBM Research, 'The Shape of Money Laundering: Subgraph Representation Learning on the Blockchain with the Elliptic2 Dataset', 2024 (arXiv:2404.19109).

### method

- **core_method**: Two successor datasets that extend the original node-level Elliptic (Elliptic1) in orthogonal directions. Elliptic++ enriches the SAME 203,769-transaction graph with a second, ACTOR/ADDRESS layer: ~822k Bitcoin wallet-address nodes (with 56 address features and 1.27M temporal address interactions) plus address-to-address and address-to-transaction bipartite graphs, and it exposes 183 transaction features (extending the original 166). This lets a model detect both illicit transactions AND illicit actors (addresses), and trace the root-cause actors behind fraudulent flows -- i.e. it adds actor-level labeling and multi-granularity graphs on top of Elliptic1's transaction-level task. Elliptic2 instead changes the ML task from node classification to SUBGRAPH classification: it provides 122,000+ labeled subgraphs (connected clusters of Bitcoin address-clusters representing candidate money-laundering flows) embedded in a very large background graph of 49.3M node-clusters and 196.2M edges (43 node features, 95 edge features). The goal is to learn the recurring 'shapes' of money laundering -- the topological/relational signature of laundering flows (e.g. gather-scatter, peeling, layering) -- and classify whether a newly extracted subgraph is suspicious. Baselines include subgraph-GNN methods (GNN-Segmented, Sub2Vec, and GLASS, which injects background-graph context and performs best).
- **graph_task_type**: Elliptic++: node (dual: transaction-level node classification AND actor/address-level node classification). Elliptic2: subgraph classification (binary: suspicious vs licit subgraph).
- **temporal_modeling**: Elliptic++ preserves Elliptic1's 49 discrete time steps for the transaction graph and adds temporal address interactions (822,942 temporal address occurrences deduplicating to ~530k train / ~90k val / ~202k test), so temporal splits carry over. Elliptic2 subgraphs are extracted from the Bitcoin transaction history over time, but the released task is framed primarily as static subgraph classification against a fixed background graph rather than a rolling temporal forecast; temporal ordering is implicit in the underlying chain rather than a first-class split dimension.
- **class_imbalance_handling**: Both remain highly imbalanced. Elliptic++ transaction layer: 4,545 illicit / 42,019 licit / 157,205 unknown (same as Elliptic1); address layer is likewise dominated by unknown addresses. Elliptic2 is extreme: of 122k labeled subgraphs only 2,763 are suspicious vs 119,047 licit (~2.3% positive). The datasets themselves do not impose a fixed imbalance remedy; they are designed so that PR-AUC / minority-class F1 are the meaningful metrics, and baselines rely on the background-graph context (GLASS) rather than resampling to recover signal.
- **heterophily_handling**: Addressed structurally rather than algorithmically. Elliptic++'s address-to-address and address-to-transaction graphs let models reason about actors that mix illicit and licit counterparties (camouflage), giving richer context to separate laundering actors from the normal neighbors they hide among. Elliptic2's premise is explicitly that laundering signal lives in subgraph SHAPE and in the background context around a subgraph -- GLASS's use of background-graph structure is essentially a heterophily/camouflage countermeasure, since an isolated node view is insufficient.
- **supervision_regime**: Supervised binary classification on labeled transactions/addresses (Elliptic++) or labeled subgraphs (Elliptic2), each embedded in a much larger unlabeled background graph (transductive / semi-supervised style, with the unlabeled majority used for structure).
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Substantially stronger than Elliptic1. Elliptic++ adds directed address-to-address and address-to-transaction edges (actor interactions), enabling money-flow tracing between wallets. Elliptic2 provides explicit edge features (95 per edge, e.g. amount/count/time attributes on the transaction edges between address-clusters) in a directed background graph, so edge attributes and flow direction are first-class inputs to subgraph models -- exactly the amount/time edge signal missing from the original Elliptic release.
- **typology_coverage**: Elliptic2 is built to capture laundering typologies as topological shapes -- gather-scatter, peeling chains, layering, and consolidation patterns connecting known illicit sources to end-point services -- because subgraphs are extracted along suspicious flows between labeled illicit origins and cash-out points. Elliptic++ covers typologies implicitly through combined transaction+actor labels (scams, ransomware, darknet markets, Ponzi) and enables root-cause actor analysis, but without an explicit per-typology taxonomy.
- **chain_coverage**: Bitcoin (single chain) for both.

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: Elliptic++ (extends Elliptic1: 203,769 tx nodes / 234,355 edges / 49 time steps, plus ~822k address nodes, 56 address features, 1.27M address interactions) and Elliptic2 (122,000+ labeled subgraphs: 119,047 licit + 2,763 suspicious, over a background graph of 49.3M node-clusters / 196.2M edges / 43 node + 95 edge features). Both are compared against the original Elliptic1 node-classification benchmark.
- **key_metrics**: Elliptic++ reports transaction- and address-level detection benchmarks (RandomForest, MLP, GCN, GAT etc.); tree ensembles again lead on the transaction task, and the added actor features improve address-level illicit detection (the paper's contribution is the dataset + multi-granularity analysis rather than a single headline F1). Elliptic2 subgraph-classification baselines (illicit/minority F1, ROC-AUC): GNN-Segmented F1 ~0.398, ROC-AUC ~0.537; Sub2Vec F1 ~0.944 but ROC-AUC ~0.496 (near-random ranking despite high F1 -- a caution on metric choice under imbalance); GLASS (background-context subgraph GNN) F1 ~0.933, ROC-AUC ~0.889 and best overall, correctly flagging ~85% of suspicious subgraphs. The central quantitative message: modeling the SHAPE of a flow (subgraph + background context) markedly outperforms treating subgraphs in isolation.

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Elliptic2's subgraph framing is inherently more explainable for investigators: a flagged unit is a whole money-flow subgraph (a set of connected addresses/transactions forming a recognizable laundering shape), which is directly presentable as evidence rather than an opaque per-node score. Elliptic++ adds explainability by linking illicit transactions back to the responsible actor/address and providing case-analysis notebooks that trace root-cause wallets. Neither ships a formal GNN-explainer, but the data granularity itself yields human-readable subgraph/actor evidence.
- **explainability_type**: subgraph-native / motif-based (Elliptic2 returns suspicious subgraphs = laundering shapes; Elliptic++ enables actor/root-cause attribution). No dedicated post-hoc explainer library.

### practicality

- **code_availability**: Open source. Elliptic++: github.com/git-disl/EllipticPlusPlus (data guide, feature descriptions, and case-analysis notebooks). Elliptic2: github.com/MITIBMxGraph/Elliptic2 (official usage guide, loaders, and GLASS/baseline references).
- **data_availability**: Publicly downloadable for research. Elliptic++ is distributed via its GitHub repo (Google Drive links). Elliptic2 (~large: tens of millions of nodes/edges) is released through the MITIBMxGraph GitHub with download instructions; both carry research/non-commercial usage terms consistent with Elliptic's data policy.
- **scalability**: Elliptic++ remains moderate (204k tx + ~822k addresses) and is CPU-feasible. Elliptic2 is large-scale: a 49.3M-node / 196.2M-edge background graph demands mini-batch / neighborhood-sampling subgraph GNNs and non-trivial GPU memory; the labeled-subgraph task keeps per-example compute small (avg subgraph ~3-4 nodes, max ~296) but loading and indexing the background graph is the real engineering cost. This makes Elliptic2 a realistic stress test for production-scale AML, whereas Elliptic++ is closer to lab scale.
- **llm_integration**: none
- **deployment_maturity**: academic benchmark datasets with open-source tooling (both produced with direct involvement of Elliptic, whose production AML products use related techniques, but the releases themselves are research benchmarks).
- **integration_difficulty**: Elliptic++ low-to-medium: same transaction backbone as Elliptic1 so existing pipelines transfer; the extra address graph needs additional joins but feature semantics are documented. Elliptic2 medium-to-high: switching from node to subgraph classification requires a different model family (subgraph GNNs like GLASS, background-context sampling, subgraph extraction/labeling logic) and infrastructure to hold a ~50M-node graph -- a larger lift for ChainLens than reusing the node-level Elliptic pipeline.

### chainlens_relevance

Elliptic++ validates ChainLens's plan to fuse transaction-level and actor/address-level views: its address-to-address and address-to-transaction graphs are exactly the kind of SNA structure ChainLens wants for tracing root-cause actors behind a suspicious flow (and it ports cleanly to a TRON TRC-20 address graph). ## Ethereum phishing and scam-token detection

### basic_info

- **name**: Ethereum phishing and scam-token detection
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2019-2025
- **authors_or_org**: Wu et al. (Trans2Vec/T-EDGE, ZJU/Zhejiang University of Technology); Wu Yueming et al. (TokenScout, Nanyang Technological University / Xi'an Jiaotong University, CCS 2024); PDHG authors (Expert Systems with Applications, 2025); DA-HGNN authors (Sensors/MDPI, 2024)

### method

- **core_method**: A lineage of address- and token-level scam detection methods on Ethereum. (1) Trans2Vec (Wu et al., 2020): models the transaction network as a weighted directed graph and performs biased random walks weighted by transaction amount and timestamp to sample node sequences, then applies skip-gram embedding + one-class SVM for phishing account classification. (2) T-EDGE (Lin et al., 2019/2020): Temporal WEighted MultiDiGraph Embedding; models Ethereum as a Temporal Weighted Multidigraph (TWMDG), performs time-respecting (temporal-order-constrained) and amount-biased random walks over a multigraph so parallel edges and time direction are preserved, feeding node embeddings to a classifier. (3) TokenScout (CCS 2024): first temporal-GNN framework for scam-TOKEN early detection; formulates token transfer data as a dynamic temporal attributed multigraph, uses a temporal graph neural network (continuous-time / memory-based TGN-style) plus a contrastive-learning graph-representation refinement module to build a discriminative risk space for rugpulls, honeypots and Ponzi tokens. (4) PDHG (ESWA 2025): heterogeneous graph transformer over a multi-type graph (accounts, exchanges, token contracts); learns implicit meta-paths via type-aware attention (no manual meta-path specification) and adds PDHGexplainer for interpretability. (5) DA-HGNN (Sensors/MDPI 2024): hybrid GNN combining data augmentation (sliding-window sampling to build time-series/temporal features) with a GAE using SAGEConv encoder to reconstruct an augmented transaction graph and fuse temporal + basic + structural embeddings.
- **graph_task_type**: Primarily node classification (address-level phishing account detection); TokenScout is subgraph/entity (token-contract-level) classification
- **temporal_modeling**: Strong and increasing emphasis. Trans2Vec uses timestamp-biased random walks; T-EDGE enforces time-respecting temporal walks over a multidigraph (edges ordered by timestamp, amount-weighted). TokenScout is fully temporal: continuous-time dynamic temporal attributed multigraph with memory/time-encoding TGN-style updates enabling EARLY detection (flags tokens before full lifecycle). DA-HGNN builds explicit time-series features via sliding-window sampling data augmentation. PDHG is comparatively more static/structural (transformer over heterogeneous snapshot).
- **supervision_regime**: Mixed: supervised (T-EDGE, PDHG, DA-HGNN, TokenScout classifier head); Trans2Vec is effectively one-class/weakly-supervised; TokenScout adds self-supervised contrastive pretraining
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Yes, and it is a distinguishing strength of this line of work. T-EDGE explicitly preserves directed multigraph structure with parallel edges, per-edge amount weights and timestamps; Trans2Vec biases walks by amount + time on a weighted directed graph; TokenScout uses a temporal attributed MULTIgraph with rich edge attributes (transfer amount, time); DA-HGNN encodes amount/time as edge/node features. PDHG uses typed directed edges within the heterogeneous graph.
- **typology_coverage**: Phishing (address-level account scams) is the dominant target for Trans2Vec/T-EDGE/PDHG/DA-HGNN. TokenScout extends to scam-TOKEN typologies: rugpulls, honeypots, and Ponzi tokens. Pig-butchering, smurfing, peeling and mixer typologies are largely out of scope for these Ethereum-graph methods (covered instead by the TRON/USDT flow-tracing literature).
- **chain_coverage**: Ethereum (ERC-20 / account transaction network). No native TRON coverage; ideas are transferable to TRON TRC-20 address scoring.

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: Ethereum phishing-account labels from Etherscan / XBlock-ETH (the community standard ~1,000-6,000 labeled phishing addresses plus sampled normal accounts, first curated by Wu et al. for Trans2Vec/T-EDGE and reused widely). TokenScout built a large custom dataset of 214,084 standard ERC-20 tokens (2015-Feb 2023) with rugpull/honeypot/Ponzi labels. PDHG and DA-HGNN use Etherscan-labeled phishing account graphs. No use of Elliptic/Elliptic++ (those are Bitcoin).
- **key_metrics**: TokenScout: 98.41% balanced accuracy; deployed Mar-May 2023 it flagged 706 rugpulls, 174 honeypots, 90 Ponzi tokens, alerting to >$240M at risk. PDHG: AUC 96.04%, recall 89.87% (reported to surpass SOTA). DA-HGNN: AUC-ROC 0.994, recall 0.995, F1 0.994 (on its augmented split — high numbers partly reflect data-augmentation/evaluation setup, treat with caution). Trans2Vec / T-EDGE: reported to outperform prior network-embedding baselines on phishing-node classification (micro/macro-F1); exact figures vary by sampled normal-node ratio. Note: none report on the Elliptic official split (different chain).

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: PDHG is the most explicit: ships PDHGexplainer, which surfaces the influential typed nodes/edges (meta-path-like evidence) behind each phishing verdict. TokenScout's contrastive risk space and temporal signals give some interpretability of when/why a token becomes risky but is not a formal explainer. Random-walk methods (Trans2Vec/T-EDGE) and DA-HGNN are largely black-box embeddings without dedicated explanation modules.
- **explainability_type**: PDHG: post-hoc GNN explainer (GNNExplainer-style subgraph/edge attribution over a heterogeneous graph). PDHG heterogeneous transformer also has attention-native signals. Others: none / attention-native only.

### practicality

- **scalability**: T-EDGE/Trans2Vec random-walk embeddings scale to hundreds of thousands of nodes on CPU but require offline walk generation; not real-time. TokenScout demonstrated production-scale, near-real-time deployment on live Ethereum (screened 214k tokens; ran continuously Mar-May 2023 for early warning), the strongest scalability/real-time evidence in this set. PDHG/DA-HGNN are GNNs trained on sampled subgraphs (moderate scale, GPU-oriented, batchable via GraphSAGE-style neighbor sampling in DA-HGNN).
- **llm_integration**: none
- **deployment_maturity**: TokenScout: closest to production (live Ethereum early-warning deployment reported in the CCS 2024 paper). Trans2Vec/T-EDGE/PDHG/DA-HGNN: academic prototypes.
- **integration_difficulty**: Medium. The random-walk embedding ideas (Trans2Vec amount+time biased walks, T-EDGE temporal multidigraph walks) are low-cost to reimplement on TRON TRC-20 data and complement ChainLens SNA features directly. TokenScout's temporal-GNN + contrastive pipeline is higher effort (needs a TGN framework, streaming ingestion, and token-lifecycle labels) but maps well onto ChainLens's evidence-narrative goal. PDHG's heterogeneous transformer + explainer is medium-high (needs typed graph schema: address/exchange/contract node types) but its explainer aligns with ChainLens evidence output.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: Highly relevant as a transfer blueprint for TRON TRC-20 address scoring. ChainLens already plans SNA features + GCN/GraphSAGE + rule motifs + evidence narratives; this lineage shows the winning ingredients: (a) treat transfers as a DIRECTED TEMPORAL MULTIGRAPH with amount+time edge features (T-EDGE) rather than a simple static graph — directly portable to TRC-20 USDT transfers; (b) use amount/time-biased random-walk embeddings (Trans2Vec) as cheap, CPU-friendly features to concatenate with hand-crafted SNA features; (c) adopt TokenScout's temporal-GNN + contrastive-refinement recipe for EARLY scam-token/address flagging and its multigraph attributed formulation for streaming detection; (d) borrow PDHG's heterogeneous-graph schema (separate node types for accounts vs exchanges vs contracts) plus its PDHGexplainer to generate the human-readable evidence narratives ChainLens wants; (e) reuse DA-HGNN's sliding-window data augmentation + GAE reconstruction to combat the extreme legit/scam imbalance that TRON TRC-20 will exhibit. The GraphSAGE (SAGEConv) encoder in DA-HGNN matches ChainLens's chosen GraphSAGE backbone, lowering integration cost.
- **key_takeaways**: Temporal + directed + multigraph modeling with amount/time edge features (T-EDGE, TokenScout) consistently beats static-graph baselines — make ChainLens's TRC-20 graph temporal and directed, not flattened.<br>TokenScout (CCS 2024) is the reference for EARLY scam-TOKEN detection (rugpull/honeypot/Ponzi) via temporal GNN + contrastive refinement, and proved production-scale live deployment on Ethereum flagging >$240M at risk — strongest engineering precedent to emulate for TRON token scoring.<br>DA-HGNN's sliding-window data augmentation + GAE/SAGEConv reconstruction is a ready-made recipe for the extreme class imbalance and heterogeneity ChainLens will face, and it already uses the GraphSAGE encoder ChainLens plans to use.<br>PDHG (ESWA 2025) shows heterogeneous graph transformers with type-aware attention remove the need for hand-designed meta-paths and ship a post-hoc explainer (PDHGexplainer) — directly supports ChainLens's evidence-narrative requirement.<br>Cheap random-walk embeddings (Trans2Vec) are trivially portable to TRC-20 and can augment ChainLens SNA features on CPU before investing in heavier GNNs; beware that DA-HGNN's near-0.99 metrics reflect augmented evaluation splits and may not transfer to real-world TRON imbalance.

## GNN Explainability for AML and Fraud (GNNExplainer / PGExplainer / SubgraphX, SEFraud self-explainable masks, and explainable GNN AML frameworks on Elliptic)

### basic_info

- **name**: GNN Explainability for AML and Fraud (GNNExplainer / PGExplainer / SubgraphX, SEFraud self-explainable masks, and explainable GNN AML frameworks on Elliptic)
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2025
- **authors_or_org**: A method family spanning several works: SEFraud (Kaidi Li et al., Ant Group / ICBC deployment, KDD 2024, arXiv:2406.11389); the canonical post-hoc explainers GNNExplainer (Ying et al., NeurIPS 2019), PGExplainer (Luo et al., NeurIPS 2020) and SubgraphX (Yuan et al., ICML 2021); an explainable GNN AML framework on the Elliptic dataset (Oluwatosin Lawal, Awele Okolie, Callistus Obunadike, IJCNC / IJNSA Vol.17 No.5-6, Nov-Dec 2025); and Explainable Fraud Detection with GNNExplainer and Shapley Values (arXiv:2509.12262, 2025).

### method

- **core_method**: Umbrella of explanation techniques attached to GNN fraud/AML classifiers, grouped into three paradigms. (1) POST-HOC PERTURBATION/ATTRIBUTION explainers applied after a trained GNN: GNNExplainer optimizes soft masks over edges and node features to find the compact subgraph + feature subset that maximizes mutual information with the prediction (per-instance, model-agnostic); PGExplainer trains a parameterized (amortized) MLP that predicts edge-importance via the Gumbel-softmax reparameterization trick, so it generalizes across instances and is inductive/faster than GNNExplainer; SubgraphX searches connected subgraphs with Monte-Carlo Tree Search and scores each candidate subgraph's contribution with Shapley values, yielding human-readable connected motifs rather than scattered edges. These are typically paired with SHAP/Shapley feature attribution on the tabular node features. (2) SELF-EXPLAINABLE (intrinsic) models, exemplified by SEFraud: a customized heterogeneous graph transformer with learnable feature masks and edge masks trained jointly with the classifier under a novel triplet loss, so the mask that drives the prediction IS the explanation (no separate post-hoc pass, far lower inference cost, explanations align with expert business rules). (3) ATTENTION-NATIVE explanations, where GAT-style attention weights over neighbors/relations are read out directly as importance scores. In the AML setting these explainers are wrapped around a base node classifier (usually a 2-layer GCN or GraphSAGE on the Elliptic transaction graph) and the resulting subgraphs are interpreted against known laundering motifs (fan-out/gather-scatter, transaction mixing, peeling).
- **graph_task_type**: node (transaction/account-level classification is the target; explanations are delivered as subgraph + feature attributions around the classified node). SubgraphX and GNNExplainer also support graph- and edge/link-level explanation.
- **class_imbalance_handling**: Handled at the base-classifier level, not by the explainer. The Elliptic explainable-AML framework tests class weighting and focal loss on the <2% illicit class but reports performance 'remains unstable' (the plain GCN even collapsed to majority-class prediction), underscoring that explainability does not fix imbalance. SEFraud frames fraud as an imbalanced task and relies on its triplet-loss mask learning plus standard weighting; the GNNExplainer+Shapley work operates on imbalanced credit-card/fraud graphs with resampling on the classifier side.
- **supervision_regime**: Base classifiers are supervised / semi-supervised (transductive on Elliptic labeled subset). Explanations are unsupervised/optimization-driven: GNNExplainer and SubgraphX need no explanation labels; PGExplainer trains its explainer network on the model's own predictions; SEFraud's masks are learned jointly and self-supervised by the triplet loss.
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Edge masks are central: GNNExplainer, PGExplainer and SEFraud all produce edge-importance scores, so directed payment edges (amount, time) can in principle be highlighted. In practice on Elliptic the directed multigraph structure is exploited only weakly because Elliptic ships precomputed aggregate node features rather than rich per-edge attributes; SubgraphX returns connected subgraphs that preserve edge structure, making direction/flow legible for an analyst even when edge features are sparse.
- **typology_coverage**: Explanations are interpreted against classic laundering typologies: the Elliptic explainable-AML framework explicitly discusses fan-out and transaction-mixing patterns surfaced by GNNExplainer subgraphs; SubgraphX/GNNExplainer motifs naturally capture gather-scatter (smurfing) and peeling-chain shapes. SEFraud (deployed at ICBC) targets malicious-account and transaction fraud typologies. No formal per-typology labels exist, so typology mapping is a human, post-hoc reading of the returned subgraph.
- **chain_coverage**: Bitcoin (Elliptic) for the AML explainability studies; SEFraud is validated on banking/e-commerce heterogeneous graphs (ICBC production) rather than a specific chain; the techniques are chain-agnostic and transfer to Ethereum/TRON transaction graphs.

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Three complementary approaches. Post-hoc SUBGRAPH extraction: GNNExplainer returns a minimal edge+feature subgraph maximizing mutual information; PGExplainer returns an amortized edge-importance map (fast, reusable across many alerts); SubgraphX returns a Shapley-scored connected subgraph (best human readability, highest compute). SELF-EXPLAINABLE MASKS: SEFraud emits learned feature/edge masks concurrent with the prediction, so every alert ships with the exact features/edges that caused it, at production-viable latency. FEATURE ATTRIBUTION: SHAP/Shapley values over the 166 tabular features to say which attributes (volume, fee, neighbor counts) drove the score. ATTENTION-NATIVE: GAT attention weights read out as neighbor importance. For AML the extracted subgraphs are then narrated against laundering typologies (fan-out, mixing, peeling) to build the compliance-report evidence chain.
- **explainability_type**: Covers the full spectrum: post-hoc (GNNExplainer, PGExplainer, SubgraphX), self-explainable mask (SEFraud interpretative feature/edge masks), SHAP-LIME (Shapley feature attribution), and attention-native (GAT). For a compliance report the most valuable are the connected-subgraph explainers (SubgraphX / self-explainable masks) that produce a legible entity-and-flow motif, plus SHAP for per-feature justification.

### practicality

- **data_availability**: Elliptic is publicly downloadable (Kaggle ellipticco/elliptic-data-set; PyTorch Geometric EllipticBitcoinDataset; research/non-commercial license). SEFraud's ICBC production data is proprietary and not released. YelpChi/Amazon fraud graphs used by benchmark explainers are public.
- **scalability**: Sharp differences by method. PGExplainer is the most scalable (amortized: train the explainer once, then near-constant-time per new alert, inductive) and CPU-feasible for real-time alert triage. GNNExplainer is per-instance optimization (seconds per node) - fine for on-demand analyst review, heavy for bulk. SubgraphX (MCTS + Shapley) is the most expensive (can be seconds-to-minutes per node) and does not scale to real-time; reserve it for high-value case deep-dives. SEFraud is explicitly engineered for production latency because the mask is computed in the forward pass, meeting real-world throughput at ICBC scale. Elliptic-scale graphs (~204k nodes) are trivial; the challenge is live chain scale.
- **llm_integration**: none (this method family is pre-LLM; LLM-narration of these subgraph explanations is a separate, complementary trend - see the LLM+graph fraud investigation agents item).
- **deployment_maturity**: Mixed. GNNExplainer/PGExplainer/SubgraphX are mature open-source libraries (PyG/DIG). SEFraud is a production system (deployed at ICBC). The Elliptic explainable-AML frameworks are academic prototypes/journal studies.
- **integration_difficulty**: low-to-medium. LOW to add GNNExplainer/PGExplainer to ChainLens: both are one import in PyTorch Geometric's Explainer API and drop directly onto ChainLens's planned GCN/GraphSAGE, immediately yielding edge+feature subgraphs for evidence narratives; SHAP on the SNA/tabular features is equally cheap. MEDIUM for SubgraphX (compute cost, needs a job-queue for expensive cases) and MEDIUM-HIGH for a self-explainable SEFraud-style model (must retrain a custom masked graph transformer, no public code, engineering the triplet-loss masks). Main friction: mapping extracted subgraphs onto ChainLens's rule/motif taxonomy so the explanation is expressed in laundering-typology terms an analyst/regulator accepts.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: Directly actionable: ChainLens already plans GCN/GraphSAGE + rule motifs + an evidence-narrative layer, and this method family supplies the missing 'why' that turns a risk score into a defensible compliance report. Concretely, ChainLens should (a) attach PGExplainer for fast, amortized edge-importance on every alert (real-time triage) and GNNExplainer for on-demand analyst deep-dives, both available out-of-the-box in PyTorch Geometric on the same GCN/GraphSAGE model; (b) reserve SubgraphX (Shapley-scored connected subgraphs) for high-value cases where a clean, human-legible flow motif is worth the compute; (c) run SHAP over the SNA/aggregate features so each alert carries a per-feature justification alongside the subgraph; (d) feed the returned subgraph directly into ChainLens's rule/motif matcher so the explanation is auto-labeled as fan-out/gather-scatter/mixing/peeling - exactly the typology language a SAR/compliance narrative needs; and (e) treat SEFraud as the north-star architecture if ChainLens later wants intrinsic, low-latency explanations at production scale. The IJCNC/IJNSA Elliptic study is a cautionary anchor: explainability does NOT rescue a mode-collapsed classifier, so ChainLens must first secure a usable illicit-F1 (class weighting, focal loss, tree-ensemble baseline) before the explanations are meaningful.
- **key_takeaways**: Use the right explainer for the right job: PGExplainer (amortized, inductive, CPU-cheap) for real-time alert triage; GNNExplainer for on-demand analyst deep-dives; SubgraphX (MCTS+Shapley) only for high-value cases because it is compute-heavy but gives the cleanest connected-subgraph motif.<br>For compliance reports the most valuable explanation types are connected-subgraph explainers (SubgraphX / self-explainable masks) that yield a legible entity-and-flow motif, plus SHAP feature attribution for a per-feature 'why' - scattered edge saliency alone is not audit-friendly.<br>SEFraud shows self-explainable (intrinsic) masks beat post-hoc explainers on latency and expert-alignment (deployed in ICBC production); it is the target architecture if ChainLens wants explanations at production scale, but there is no public code so expect to reimplement the masked graph transformer + triplet loss.<br>Auto-map every extracted subgraph onto ChainLens's rule/motif taxonomy (fan-out, gather-scatter, mixing, peeling) so explanations are expressed in laundering-typology terms regulators accept - GNNExplainer/PGExplainer/SubgraphX/SEFraud are all one import in PyTorch Geometric / DIG for the post-hoc ones.<br>Explainability is not a substitute for a working classifier: the Elliptic explainable-AML study collapsed to majority class despite class weighting/focal loss, so fix illicit-class F1/PR-AUC first (weighting, focal loss, tree-ensemble baseline, temporal split), then explain.

## Heterophily and camouflage-aware fraud GNNs (CARE-GNN / PC-GNN -> SEC-GFD, DGA-GNN, GDN/GAGA, SCFCRC)

### basic_info

- **name**: Heterophily and camouflage-aware fraud GNNs (CARE-GNN / PC-GNN -> SEC-GFD, DGA-GNN, GDN/GAGA, SCFCRC)
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2020-2025
- **authors_or_org**: Synthesis of a research line: CARE-GNN (Dou, Liu, Sun et al., CIKM 2020); PC-GNN (Liu et al., WWW 2021); GDN & GAGA (group-aggregation / heterophily-shift works); SEC-GFD (Fan Xu, Nan Wang, Hao Wu, Xuezhi Wen, Xibin Zhao, arXiv 2312.06441, AAAI 2024); DGA-GNN (Mingjiang Duan, Tongya Zheng et al., AAAI 2024); SCFCRC (Xiaocheng Zhang, Zhuangzhuang Ye, Guoping Zhao, Jianing Wang, Xiaohong Su, arXiv 2501.12430, 2025); BWGNN, DHMP and related spectral/heterophily works

### method

- **core_method**: A generation of GNN fraud detectors that reject the homophily assumption underlying vanilla GCN/GraphSAGE (which act as low-pass filters and smooth fraudsters into their benign neighbors). The line evolves in three stages. (1) Neighbor-selection / camouflage-resistant (2020-2021): CARE-GNN uses a reinforcement-learning-tuned similarity measure to filter/select camouflaged neighbors across multiple relations (counters feature + relation camouflage); PC-GNN ('Pick and Choose') adds a label-balanced sampler to pick nodes and a neighborhood sampler to choose informative neighbors, jointly fixing camouflage and class imbalance. (2) Spectral / heterophily-aware (2022-2024): recognizing that heterophily shifts spectral energy toward high frequencies, methods add band-pass/high-pass filters -- BWGNN (Beta wavelet band-pass), SEC-GFD (hybrid filter splitting the spectrum into mixed frequency bands per the correlation between spectral energy distribution and heterophily, plus a local environmental constraint module for label utilization), GDN (separates anomaly-pattern vs neighborhood-pattern features to counter train/test heterophily shift), GAGA (group aggregation + learnable encoding for low-homophily graphs). (3) Grouping + joint camouflage counteraction (2024-2025): DGA-GNN uses decision-tree binning to encode non-additive node attributes into bin vectors and a feedback dynamic grouping strategy with hierarchical aggregation to separate discriminative groups; SCFCRC is a Transformer-based detector that simultaneously counters feature camouflage (a Feature Camouflage Filter using label-propagation pseudo-labels + instance/prototype contrastive learning) and relation camouflage (a Relation Camouflage Refiner), rather than treating them separately.
- **graph_task_type**: Node classification (binary: fraud vs benign) on multi-relation / attributed graphs
- **class_imbalance_handling**: A core theme. PC-GNN's label-balanced sampler explicitly oversamples the minority fraud class and undersamples majority neighbors; CARE-GNN uses relation-aware neighbor selection that indirectly helps; general line uses AUC / F1-macro / GMean as imbalance-robust metrics; some works add focal/weighted losses and pseudo-label propagation (SCFCRC) to expand usable minority signal.
- **heterophily_handling**: THE defining contribution of this line. Approaches: (a) neighbor filtering/selection to drop camouflage edges (CARE-GNN, PC-GNN choose); (b) spectral band-pass/high-pass filtering to keep the high-frequency signal that distinguishes a fraud node from its dissimilar neighbors (BWGNN, SEC-GFD hybrid filter, GDN, DHMP dual-channel homophilic/heterophilic split); (c) grouping so that messages from similar vs dissimilar neighbors are aggregated separately (DGA-GNN dynamic grouping, GAGA group aggregation); (d) joint feature+relation de-camouflage (SCFCRC). The shared insight: fraud graphs are heterophilic because fraudsters deliberately connect to many benign users, so low-pass smoothing destroys the signal.
- **supervision_regime**: Semi-supervised node classification (small labeled fraud set + large unlabeled); SCFCRC and label-propagation variants add pseudo-labeling / weak supervision; self-supervised contrastive components appear in SCFCRC and unsupervised heterophily-guided variants
- **typology_coverage**: Typology-agnostic: this line targets the general statistical signature of camouflaged fraud (heterophilic connectivity) rather than named laundering typologies like smurfing or peeling. It complements, rather than replaces, typology/motif detectors.
- **chain_coverage**: Mostly benchmark/off-chain fraud graphs (YelpChi review fraud, Amazon-Fraud); Elliptic (Bitcoin) and financial datasets (T-Finance) also used; principles transfer to Ethereum/TRON account graphs

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: YelpChi (review fraud), Amazon-Fraud, T-Finance and T-Social (SEC-GFD, GDN, GAGA), Elliptic (Bitcoin) in some variants; DGA-GNN evaluated on five datasets; CARE-GNN/PC-GNN standardized on Yelp + Amazon

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Mostly implicit. Explanations come from which neighbors/relations were filtered (CARE-GNN, PC-GNN keep/drop decisions), which spectral bands carry the anomaly signal (SEC-GFD, BWGNN), or which group a node was assigned to (DGA-GNN). These are more mechanism-level than human-narrative; none produce typology narratives. Post-hoc explainers (GNNExplainer/PGExplainer) can be layered on but are not built in.
- **explainability_type**: attention-native / mechanism-level (neighbor-selection scores, spectral-band attribution, grouping decisions); no built-in rule/narrative or SHAP-LIME

### practicality

- **data_availability**: YelpChi, Amazon-Fraud, T-Finance, T-Social, and Elliptic are all publicly downloadable standard benchmarks (research licenses)
- **llm_integration**: none (though later DGP-style dual-granularity prompting explores LLM/prompt framing for fraud, outside this core set)
- **deployment_maturity**: Academic prototypes with clean open-source reference implementations (CARE-GNN, PC-GNN, SEC-GFD, DGA-GNN); widely used as baselines but not turnkey production systems
- **integration_difficulty**: Medium for ChainLens. The heterophily insight is directly transferable and the reference code is available, but ChainLens's directed, amount/time-weighted financial graph differs from the multi-relation review graphs these methods target, so relation construction and adapting to directed edge features needs work. Swapping ChainLens's low-pass GCN/GraphSAGE for a band-pass/heterophily-aware backbone (BWGNN/SEC-GFD) is a moderate, well-scoped change; adding neighbor filtering (CARE/PC-GNN) is low effort.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: Directly relevant to ChainLens's GCN/GraphSAGE node-scoring layer. Crypto laundering accounts deliberately transact with many normal wallets, making the graph heterophilic exactly as this line assumes, so plain GraphSAGE (low-pass) under-detects camouflaged actors. ChainLens should (a) replace or augment its low-pass backbone with a spectral band-pass filter (BWGNN or SEC-GFD's hybrid filter) to retain the high-frequency signal that separates a launderer from its benign neighbors; (b) adopt PC-GNN's label-balanced + neighbor-choosing sampler to jointly handle Elliptic/TRON's extreme illicit/licit imbalance and camouflage; (c) consider DGA-GNN's dynamic grouping / decision-tree binning to handle non-additive on-chain features (balances, tx counts). These upgrade the learning layer, while the money-laundering-motif line supplies the explainable rule layer -- together they form the rule+learning hybrid ChainLens targets.
- **key_takeaways**: Fraud graphs are heterophilic by design (fraudsters link to benign users); vanilla GCN/GraphSAGE are low-pass filters that smooth this signal away -- ChainLens's current backbone likely underperforms on camouflaged actors.<br>Two complementary fixes: neighbor selection/filtering (CARE-GNN, PC-GNN) and spectral band-pass filtering (BWGNN, SEC-GFD) to keep high-frequency discriminative signal; SEC-GFD reports beating GDN/BWGNN/H2-FDetector.<br>PC-GNN's label-balanced sampler kills two birds -- camouflage AND extreme class imbalance -- which is exactly ChainLens's Elliptic/TRON setting; open-source and low-effort to adopt.<br>2024-2025 frontier (DGA-GNN dynamic grouping + decision-tree binning; SCFCRC joint feature+relation de-camouflage) shows separating similar/dissimilar neighbor messages and countering both camouflage types together beats handling them in isolation.<br>This line boosts detection accuracy but is weak on explainability and on directed amount/time edge features -- pair it with the typology/motif line for narrative evidence and financial-flow semantics.

## IBM AMLworld benchmark and Multi-GNN (Provably Powerful GNNs for Directed Multigraphs)

### basic_info

- **name**: IBM AMLworld benchmark and Multi-GNN (Provably Powerful GNNs for Directed Multigraphs)
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2023-2024 (AMLworld dataset: NeurIPS 2023 D&B; Multi-GNN: AAAI 2024; arXiv 2306.11586 and 2306.16424 both June 2023)
- **authors_or_org**: Beni Egressy, Luc von Niederhausern, Jovan Blanusa, Erik Altman, Roger Wattenhofer, Kubilay Atasu et al., IBM Research (Zurich) and ETH Zurich

### method

- **core_method**: Two coupled contributions. (1) AMLworld: a multi-agent synthetic financial-transaction generator that simulates a virtual economy in which a subset of agents are criminals laundering illicit income through the full placement-layering-integration cycle, producing complete ground-truth labels for both individual laundering transactions and full laundering patterns/subgraphs. (2) Multi-GNN: a set of simple, model-agnostic adaptations that make standard message-passing GNNs provably powerful on DIRECTED MULTIGRAPHS. The four adaptations are: (a) reverse message passing - add reverse-direction edges so a node aggregates from both incoming and outgoing neighbours; (b) ego-IDs - a binary node feature marking the center node of each sampled computation subgraph so the network can recognise itself and detect directed cycles; (c) multigraph port numbering - deterministically number parallel edges between the same node pair so repeated/parallel transfers are distinguishable; (d) edge updates via MLPs (EMLPs) - learnable per-edge representations updated each layer so edge features (amount, timestamp, currency) propagate. The paper proves that combining reverse MP + ego-IDs + ports lets a message-passing GNN detect ANY directed subgraph pattern (e.g. cycles, scatter-gather, fan-in/fan-out), which standard GNNs provably cannot. Adaptations are applied on top of GIN, GAT, PNA and RGCN backbones.
- **graph_task_type**: edge-link classification (edge-level / transaction-level laundering detection); the theory also targets directed subgraph/motif detection
- **temporal_modeling**: Time is treated primarily as an edge feature (transaction timestamp) rather than via a dedicated recurrent/continuous-time temporal module. Data is split temporally (past for train, future for test) and port numbering plus edge-MLPs let repeated timed transfers and their ordering influence the edge representation, but there is no explicit RNN/TGN-style memory. Follow-up IBM work (Pattern-GNN / GraphMuse) adds richer temporal/pattern subgraph features.
- **class_imbalance_handling**: Extreme licit/illicit imbalance is handled at the data level (AMLworld provides HI = higher-illicit and LI = lower-illicit variants) and at the metric level (evaluation uses minority-class F1 rather than accuracy). Training typically uses class-weighted cross-entropy; the architectural gains (reverse MP + ports) are what most improve minority-class recall rather than resampling.
- **heterophily_handling**: Not addressed as an explicit heterophily mechanism; instead the directed-multigraph adaptations let the model recognise structural laundering motifs (cycles, scatter-gather) that distinguish illicit edges even when launderers transact with many legitimate accounts. Motif/subgraph awareness is the implicit camouflage-resistance mechanism.
- **supervision_regime**: supervised (full transaction-level labels supplied by the synthetic ground truth or Kaggle AML export)
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Yes - this is the central point. Directed multigraph structure (parallel edges, edge direction) and edge features (amount, timestamp, currency, payment format) are explicitly exploited via reverse message passing, port numbering and edge-MLP updates. This is precisely what standard undirected message-passing GNNs miss.
- **typology_coverage**: AMLworld models multiple laundering typologies with ground-truth pattern labels: fan-in, fan-out, scatter-gather, gather-scatter, simple/cyclic laundering cycles, bipartite and stacked patterns; smurfing-like structuring is covered through fan-out/fan-in over many accounts. Multi-GNN's provable subgraph-detection targets exactly these directed motifs.
- **chain_coverage**: Synthetic (AMLworld generator, bank-to-bank fiat transaction world, multi-currency); not a specific blockchain. Applicable methodology to on-chain directed multigraphs (Bitcoin/Ethereum/TRON) by analogy.

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: AMLworld synthetic AML datasets, released as HI (higher illicit) and LI (lower illicit) groups, each in Small/Medium/Large sizes (Large ~175M-180M transactions; Small ~ hundreds of thousands of accounts and millions of transactions over ~10 days). Distributed via Kaggle ('IBM Transactions for Anti Money Laundering'). Multi-GNN experiments also run on a second real financial-crime dataset from IBM.
- **key_metrics**: On AML Small_HI, applying the adaptations lifts the minority-class (illicit) F1 of a GIN from 28.7% to 57.2% (~+28.5 absolute, reported as up to ~30% improvement). Reverse MP + port numbering account for almost all of the gain (28.7% -> 56.9%); ego-IDs add little on this dataset. Other AML datasets show overall GIN minority-F1 gains of ~14.2%, 14.0% and 10.7%. Gains generalise across GIN/GAT/PNA backbones; PNA + adaptations is typically the strongest. Synthetic directed-subgraph-detection tasks reach near-perfect accuracy.

### practicality

- **code_availability**: Open source, Apache-2.0. Multi-GNN: https://github.com/IBM/Multi-GNN (GIN/GAT/PNA/RGCN with --reverse_mp --ego --ports --emlps flags). Related IBM repos: github.com/IBM/Pattern-GNN and the AMLworld generator/data assets.
- **data_availability**: Publicly downloadable. AMLworld datasets are hosted on Kaggle ('IBM Transactions for Anti Money Laundering', CC-BY-style / research use); preprocessing via format_kaggle_files.py in the Multi-GNN repo. The synthetic generator itself was released by IBM.
- **llm_integration**: none
- **deployment_maturity**: open-source research library / benchmark (academic prototype released by an industrial lab, IBM Research); not a turnkey production system
- **integration_difficulty**: Medium. The adaptations (reverse MP, ego-IDs, ports, edge-MLPs) are conceptually simple and framework-agnostic (PyTorch Geometric), so porting them onto ChainLens's existing GCN/GraphSAGE edge pipeline is tractable. Main effort: reshaping ChainLens graphs into a directed multigraph with per-edge features, implementing port numbering / reverse edges, and switching from node- to edge-level classification. AMLworld is fiat/synthetic, so domain transfer to TRON/Elliptic on-chain data requires feature mapping.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: Highly relevant and arguably the single most actionable method for ChainLens's edge-level laundering detection. ChainLens already blends SNA features + GCN/GraphSAGE + rule motifs; Multi-GNN shows that three cheap, provable adaptations (reverse message passing, ego-IDs, multigraph port numbering) plus edge-MLPs turn a plain GNN into a directed-multigraph model that can natively recognise the very laundering motifs (cycles, scatter-gather, fan-in/out, peeling-like chains) ChainLens currently detects with hand-written rules. On-chain transaction graphs (TRON TRC-20, Ethereum) are literally directed multigraphs with parallel timestamped/amount-weighted transfers, so the adaptations map directly. AMLworld also gives ChainLens a large, fully-labelled synthetic benchmark to pretrain/validate edge classifiers before applying to sparsely-labelled Elliptic/TRON data, and its ground-truth typology labels can supervise or evaluate ChainLens's motif/narrative layer.
- **key_takeaways**: Add reverse message passing + multigraph port numbering to ChainLens's GNN first - they delivered almost the entire ~30% minority-F1 jump (GIN 28.7% -> 56.9%) at low cost; ego-IDs help mainly when explicit cycle detection matters.<br>Move to edge-level (transaction) classification on a DIRECTED MULTIGRAPH with per-edge features (amount, timestamp, currency) via edge-MLPs - this is the structural fit for TRON/Ethereum transfer graphs.<br>Use AMLworld (Kaggle, Apache-2.0 code) as a large labelled pretraining/benchmarking corpus and as ground truth for laundering typologies (fan-in/out, scatter-gather, cycles) to validate ChainLens's rule motifs.<br>The method is provably able to detect any directed subgraph pattern, giving a principled ML replacement/complement for ChainLens's hand-coded motif rules - and pairs naturally with the evidence-narrative layer via known typology labels.<br>Watch the gap: AMLworld is synthetic fiat data with no dedicated explainability or streaming inference, and large graphs need a GPU; domain adaptation to real on-chain data and CPU/real-time constraints must be engineered.

## LLM + Graph Fraud Investigation Agents (RiskTagger annotation agent, UniDetect cross-chain LLM detection, MLED multi-level LLM-enhanced graph fraud detection, LLM-GNN soft-prompt frameworks)

### basic_info

- **name**: LLM + Graph Fraud Investigation Agents (RiskTagger annotation agent, UniDetect cross-chain LLM detection, MLED multi-level LLM-enhanced graph fraud detection, LLM-GNN soft-prompt frameworks)
- **item_type**: trend
- **year**: 2026
- **authors_or_org**: A 2025-2026 research wave combining LLMs with transaction graphs: RiskTagger (Dan Lin, Yanli Ding, Weipeng Zou, Jiachi Chen, Xiapu Luo, Jiajing Wu, Zibin Zheng; Sun Yat-sen Univ / PolyU; arXiv:2510.17848, 2025); UniDetect (Shuyi Miao et al.; arXiv:2604.12329, 2026); MLED 'Can LLMs Find Fraudsters? Multi-level LLM Enhanced Graph Fraud Detection' (Tairan Huang, Yili Wang, Qiutong Li, Changlong He, Jianliang Gao; Central South Univ / HKUST-Guangzhou; arXiv:2507.11997, ACM MM 2025); plus LLM-GNN soft-prompt frameworks such as LGSPF 'Let Relations Speak' (arXiv:2605.28524) and GSPELL post-hoc LLM explanations (arXiv:2508.07117 'From Nodes to Narratives').

### method

- **core_method**: A trend, not one system, unified by using LLMs to inject semantics, weak labels, or narrative on top of graph fraud detection, in four patterns. (1) ANNOTATION/WEAK-SUPERVISION AGENT (RiskTagger): a three-module agent - Key-clue Extractor (two-stage LLM prompting turns unstructured incident reports into structured JSON), Laundering Tracer (iteratively fetches on-chain data and runs LLM risk reasoning over multi-chain paths using Qwen3-Max with chain-of-thought + self-reflection, scored against four FATF red-flag dimensions), and Dataset Explainer (auditor-friendly narrative reports) - producing High/Medium/Low/None labels with evidence to bootstrap AML datasets. (2) LLM-DRIVEN CROSS-CHAIN DETECTION (UniDetect): three LLM agents (forensic-analysis agent writing transaction summaries, discriminative-summary analyst extracting task-relevant features, residual analyst isolating non-discriminative content) plus a Dual-PGNN graph encoder, trained by a two-stage alternating strategy that fine-tunes the LLM with LoRA and reinforcement learning (task loss -> RL reward) to enable zero-shot cross-chain transfer. (3) MULTI-LEVEL LLM-ENHANCED GNN (MLED): a type-level enhancer (LLM generates semantic representations of node TYPES, learns adaptive weights) and a relation-level enhancer (LLM embeds relation descriptions, attention-weights relations by importance); GPT-4 generates the descriptions, text-embedding-ada-002 vectorizes them, MLP reduces dimension, and these enrich a multi-relation heterogeneous GNN - prompt-based, no per-node text needed. (4) LLM-GNN SOFT PROMPTS (LGSPF/GSPELL): a GNN encoder turns multi-relational topology into 'graph tokens' fed to a frozen LLM as soft prompts, bridging structure and semantic space for detection (LGSPF) or natural-language explanation (GSPELL projects GNN embeddings into LLM token space to narrate why a node is fraud).
- **graph_task_type**: node (account/transaction fraud classification) for UniDetect, MLED, LGSPF; RiskTagger is best described as edge/path-level reasoning + annotation over multi-chain transaction paths rather than a single-node classifier; GSPELL is node-level explanation.
- **heterophily_handling**: Central to this trend: MLED is explicitly motivated by fraudster camouflage that violates homophily, using LLM-derived relation-importance attention to separate disguised fraudsters from benign look-alikes; UniDetect's residual analyst isolates non-discriminative content to stop the model from being fooled by camouflaged patterns; LGSPF's multi-relational graph tokens aim to expose relational signals fraudsters hide behind. LLM semantics act as an extra channel that camouflage in the graph topology cannot easily corrupt.
- **supervision_regime**: Spans the spectrum: RiskTagger is weakly-supervised / zero-label annotation (it CREATES labels); UniDetect is semi-supervised node classification with RL-based LLM fine-tuning and demonstrates zero-shot cross-chain transfer; MLED and LGSPF are supervised with LLM-enhanced features; GSPELL is an unsupervised/post-hoc explanation layer.
- **edge_and_direction_support**: RiskTagger and UniDetect exploit directed multi-chain transaction paths and fund-flow direction directly (path tracing, bridge/cross-chain hops, DEX swaps). MLED and LGSPF operate on multi-relation heterogeneous graphs where relation TYPE (and its LLM-generated description) is first-class, though amount/time edge attributes are used only as far as the underlying GNN encodes them. Direction is best exploited by the agentic tracers (RiskTagger's Connector tool follows bridges), weaker in the classifier-style methods.
- **typology_coverage**: Broadest of any item in this survey. RiskTagger covers the full FATF placement/layering/integration cycle including DEX swaps, token conversions, cross-chain bridge transfers, and mixer interactions (validated on the $1.5B Bybit Hack). UniDetect covers phishing, Ponzi/Ponzi schemes, gambling, honeypots, and money laundering across heterogeneous chains. MLED/LGSPF target generic review/e-commerce/financial fraud. Mixer, cross-chain bridging, and pig-butchering-style layering are explicitly reachable via the LLM narrative agents.
- **chain_coverage**: Genuinely multi-chain. RiskTagger targets EVM chains (Ethereum, BSC, Polygon) with cross-chain bridge tracking via its Connector tool. UniDetect is explicitly cross-chain, evaluated on Ethereum and Bitcoin (and an Instagram social graph for cross-domain transfer), with 94.58% zero-shot fraud recall when trained on one chain and tested on another. MLED uses Amazon/YelpChi/T-Finance/T-Social (not on-chain). LGSPF/GSPELL are chain-agnostic fraud-graph methods.

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Narrative-first, which is the trend's key selling point for compliance. RiskTagger's Dataset Explainer emits structured auditor-friendly reports: transaction-level evidence, accounts grouped by risk layer, and natural-language pattern summaries mapped to FATF red flags. UniDetect uses LLM 'transaction summaries' as forensic clues, with case studies separating discriminative from residual/hallucinated content. GSPELL generates fine-grained natural-language explanations by treating GNN embeddings as soft prompts to an LLM (no handcrafted templates or saliency masks). MLED's contribution is semantic type/relation weighting rather than end-user narrative. Net: LLMs convert subgraph/feature evidence into readable SAR-style prose.
- **explainability_type**: Primarily narrative/LLM-generated (a new category beyond the classic GNN taxonomy): RiskTagger and GSPELL produce natural-language, evidence-linked explanations; UniDetect produces LLM summaries as forensic clues. Some attention-native signal in MLED (relation attention). Not post-hoc mask explainers in the GNNExplainer sense - instead LLM narration on top of graph evidence.

### practicality

- **scalability**: RiskTagger scales by automating human annotation (agentic, API-driven; throughput bounded by LLM latency and on-chain data fetches, not graph size) - ideal for on-demand case investigation, not bulk real-time scoring. UniDetect demonstrates large scale (Bitcoin-L: 20M nodes / 203M edges) but LoRA fine-tuning + RL is GPU-heavy at training time; inference cost is dominated by per-node LLM summarization, so real-time bulk scoring is expensive. MLED/LGSPF pre-compute LLM embeddings once (per type/relation or per node) then run a normal GNN, so inference is cheap and CPU-feasible after the one-time LLM pass. Real-time capability: MLED-style (embed-once) is deployable; agentic RiskTagger/UniDetect are better for investigation than line-rate screening.
- **llm_integration**: end-to-end (spanning the trend): annotation (RiskTagger weak-supervision labels), feature-generation (MLED type/relation embeddings, LGSPF soft prompts), and end-to-end detection+narrative (UniDetect LLM+GNN with RL fine-tuning; GSPELL explanation).
- **deployment_maturity**: Academic prototypes with one open-source tool. RiskTagger is a released open-source agent (most mature/usable). UniDetect, MLED, LGSPF, GSPELL are research papers (2025-2026) without confirmed production deployments.
- **integration_difficulty**: medium. ## Low-label and imbalance strategies on Elliptic

### basic_info

- **name**: Low-label and imbalance strategies on Elliptic
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2020-2025
- **authors_or_org**: Synthesis across multiple works: Inspection-L (Lo, Kulatilleke, Sarhan, Layeghy, Portmann, Univ. of Queensland, 2022/2023); GraphSMOTE (Zhao, Zhang, Wang, ACM WSDM 2021); Lorenz et al. 'Machine learning methods to detect money laundering ... in the presence of label scarcity' (Feedzai, ACM AI in Finance 2020); LaundroGraph (Cardoso, Saleiro, Bizarro, Feedzai, 2022); Weber et al. 'Anti-Money Laundering in Bitcoin' (IBM/MIT-IBM Watson AI Lab, 2019)

### method

- **core_method**: A family of strategies for learning to flag illicit nodes on the Elliptic Bitcoin transaction graph when illicit labels are scarce (~2% of nodes, ~23% labelled overall). (1) Self-supervised representation learning: Inspection-L pretrains node embeddings with Deep Graph Infomax (DGI) over a Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) encoder using a mutual-information contrastive objective that needs no labels, then concatenates the embeddings with the 166 handcrafted Elliptic features and classifies with a Random Forest. LaundroGraph uses a self-supervised link-prediction/contrastive pretext on a bipartite customer-transaction graph. (2) Semi-supervised GNNs: standard GCN/GAT/Skip-GCN propagate the small labelled signal through the graph (label propagation effect), and EvolveGCN/temporal variants add recurrence. (3) Class-imbalance handling at the classifier/loss level: inverse-frequency class weighting in the cross-entropy loss (the original Weber GCN uses a 0.3/0.7 weighting), focal loss to down-weight easy licit examples, and graph-aware oversampling such as GraphSMOTE which interpolates synthetic minority nodes in the GNN embedding space and trains an edge generator to wire them into the graph. (4) Active learning: iteratively query an oracle for the most informative (uncertain / high-expected-model-change) unlabelled nodes; Lorenz et al. show ~5% of labels queried this way can approach fully-supervised performance. These are complementary and are often stacked (e.g. self-supervised pretraining + weighted/focal loss + active-learning label acquisition).
- **graph_task_type**: node classification (transaction-node illicit/licit); GraphSMOTE and DGI operate at node level; some variants use link-prediction as a self-supervised pretext
- **temporal_modeling**: Elliptic is a sequence of 49 time-step graph snapshots. Most imbalance/low-label methods (Inspection-L, GraphSMOTE) are static and ignore time; temporally-aware alternatives (EvolveGCN, Temporal-GCN/LSTM, wavelet-temporal graph transformers) model snapshot evolution with RNN/attention. A well-known failure mode is the 'dark market shutdown' at time step 43 after which all methods degrade, motivating temporal robustness and continual/online label acquisition.
- **class_imbalance_handling**: This is the central theme. Options benchmarked in the literature: (a) cost-sensitive learning - inverse-frequency class weights in cross-entropy (Weber GCN uses ~0.3 weight on licit); (b) focal loss to focus gradient on hard/minority illicit nodes; (c) resampling - random over/under-sampling and, graph-aware, GraphSMOTE which synthesises minority nodes in embedding space plus an edge predictor (and GraphSHA/GraphENS as newer harder-sample synthesizers); (d) representation-level mitigation - self-supervised pretraining (DGI/contrastive) yields embeddings that separate the minority class better without touching labels, then a robust downstream classifier (Random Forest) that itself tolerates imbalance well. Empirically Random Forest on the 166 features is a very strong imbalance baseline (illicit F1 ~0.83), and self-supervised embeddings mainly help recall/robustness rather than dramatically beating RF.
- **supervision_regime**: Primarily self-supervised (Inspection-L DGI, LaundroGraph) and semi-supervised (GCN label propagation on ~23% labelled nodes); plus weakly-supervised / active-learning regimes (query-efficient labelling). Downstream classification stage is supervised on the scarce labels.
- **chain_coverage**: Bitcoin (Elliptic / Elliptic++ / Elliptic2). GraphSMOTE and focal loss are chain-agnostic and transfer to Ethereum/TRON graphs. LaundroGraph and Lorenz et al. also use bank/financial-transaction graphs.

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: Elliptic Bitcoin dataset (203,769 nodes, 234,355 edges, 49 time steps, 166 features, ~2% illicit / ~21% licit / ~77% unknown). Related: Elliptic++, Elliptic2, AMLworld/IBM synthetic AML, and Feedzai proprietary bank data (LaundroGraph, Lorenz et al.). GraphSMOTE itself is benchmarked on Cora/BlogCatalog/Twitter but is method-transferable to Elliptic.
- **key_metrics**: On the official Elliptic temporal test split, illicit-class F1 is the headline metric. Weber et al. baselines: Random Forest (all features) illicit F1 ~0.83 (Precision ~0.96, Recall ~0.67); GCN illicit F1 ~0.42-0.55; Skip-GCN ~0.62; EvolveGCN illicit F1 ~0.77. Inspection-L (GIN+DGI embeddings + Random Forest, AF+DNE variant) reports illicit F1 ~0.828 with Recall ~0.721 and high precision, and micro-avg F1 around 0.97; the LF+DNE variant reports illicit F1 ~0.712 / Recall ~0.797. Active learning (Lorenz et al.) approaches fully-supervised performance using ~5% of labels. Note: all methods drop sharply after time step 43 (dark-market shutdown).

### explainability

- **explainability_type**: SHAP-LIME / feature-attribution via the tree-based head (Inspection-L); post-hoc (GNNExplainer) or attention-native in related variants; none is built into GraphSMOTE/focal-loss/weighting themselves

### practicality

- **data_availability**: Elliptic dataset is publicly downloadable (Kaggle / PyG EllipticBitcoinDataset loader) under Elliptic's research license; Elliptic++ and Elliptic2 public via their GitHub/Google-Cloud releases; AMLworld/IBM synthetic on Kaggle. Feedzai bank data (LaundroGraph, Lorenz) is proprietary/not public.
- **scalability**: Elliptic (~204k nodes / 234k edges) trains comfortably on a single GPU and is CPU-feasible for the Random-Forest head; DGI pretraining is a one-off cost. GraphSMOTE adds an edge-generation step that scales roughly O(n^2) within minority neighbourhoods, so it is heavier on large graphs. All are batch/offline; none is inherently real-time, though inference (embedding lookup + RF/GNN forward pass) is fast enough for near-real-time scoring on graphs of this size.
- **llm_integration**: none
- **deployment_maturity**: academic prototype (Inspection-L, GraphSMOTE, Lorenz active learning); LaundroGraph is an industrial research prototype at Feedzai. No turnkey production library; components (focal loss, class weighting, RF) are production-grade primitives.
- **integration_difficulty**: low-to-medium for ChainLens. Class weighting and focal loss are drop-in changes to the GCN/GraphSAGE loss (low). DGI/contrastive self-supervised pretraining is a moderate add (extra encoder + pretext objective, but reuses the same PyG graph). GraphSMOTE is medium (needs the synthetic-node + edge-generator module and careful tuning to avoid injecting noise). Active learning is medium (needs an oracle/analyst-in-the-loop labelling workflow). All are compatible with ChainLens's existing SNA-features + GCN/GraphSAGE stack on Elliptic.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: Directly actionable. ChainLens already runs SNA features + GCN/GraphSAGE on Elliptic and plans TRON TRC-20 coverage where labels are even scarcer than Elliptic's 2%. The recommended layered recipe: (1) add inverse-frequency class weighting or focal loss to the existing GCN/GraphSAGE loss as the cheapest immediate win; (2) add a DGI/contrastive self-supervised pretraining stage (Inspection-L style) so the model learns useful structure from unlabelled TRON transactions before touching the tiny labelled set, then feed embeddings + SNA features into GraphSAGE or a Random-Forest head (RF is a strong, imbalance-tolerant, explainable baseline that pairs naturally with ChainLens's evidence-narrative goal via feature importance/SHAP); (3) use GraphSMOTE cautiously to rebalance during fine-tuning; (4) stand up an active-learning loop so analyst labelling effort on new TRON cases is spent on the most informative nodes (Lorenz shows ~5% labels can near-match full supervision). This maps cleanly onto ChainLens's rule-motif + evidence-narrative pipeline: RF/SHAP attributions and the active-learning 'why this node was queried' both become narrative evidence.
- **key_takeaways**: - With ~2% illicit labels, do NOT rely on plain GCN cross-entropy: add class weighting or focal loss first (cheap, immediate recall gains). - Self-supervised pretraining (DGI/GIN, Inspection-L) is the highest-leverage move for label-scarce chains like TRON: it turns unlabelled transactions into useful representations, then a Random Forest head gives strong illicit F1 (~0.83) plus built-in feature-importance explainability. - Random Forest on good features/embeddings is a deceptively strong, imbalance-robust baseline - beat it before adopting anything fancier. - GraphSMOTE and other graph oversamplers help but can inject noisy synthetic edges; validate on the illicit-class F1, not accuracy. - Invest in an active-learning labelling loop (~5% informative labels ~ full supervision) and watch for temporal drift (Elliptic step-43 collapse) - retrain/relabel as new laundering patterns appear. - Always report illicit-class F1 / PR-AUC on the official temporal split, never overall accuracy (98% licit makes accuracy meaningless).

## Money-laundering motif and typology detection (SNA / temporal-motif line: LAS-GNN, temporal crypto motifs, smurfing gather-scatter, FlowScope, peeling chains)

### basic_info

- **name**: Money-laundering motif and typology detection (SNA / temporal-motif line: LAS-GNN, temporal crypto motifs, smurfing gather-scatter, FlowScope, peeling chains)
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2020-2025
- **authors_or_org**: Synthesis of several groups: LAS-GNN (ICAIF 2025, extends Egressy et al. AAAI 2024 / IBM AMLworld line); Sci. Reports 2024 temporal-motif study (crypto networks); FlowScope/CubeFlow (Xiangfeng Li, Shenghua Liu et al., CAS/spartan2, AAAI 2020); SMoTeF (Applied Intelligence 2024); GARG-AML (2025); peeling-chain forensics (Forensic Science International / Merkle Science / Chainalysis-style analysts)

### method

- **core_method**: A family of structure-and-flow-based methods that map money-laundering typologies onto graph motifs and dense-flow patterns, spanning three sub-lines. (1) Learning-based temporal-motif GNNs: LAS-GNN redefines a temporal graph motif aligned with the flow of funds and uses a directed GNN message-passing scheme that is timestamp-aware, with an LSTM aggregator whose permutation sensitivity encodes the temporal order of node interactions, achieving SOTA on temporal-motif finding (extends Egressy et al.'s multi-GNN AML work). (2) Combinatorial dense-flow detection: FlowScope models transactions as a multipartite graph across account layers and defines an anomalousness metric for high-volume transfer flows that leave little money behind in intermediary accounts, tracing money from source to destination along paths; CubeFlow generalizes this to coupled tensors (source-middle-destination x time). (3) Rule/heuristic + statistical motif mining: temporal-motif counting (sequences of ~3 transactions over up to 3 users) for anomaly detection and activity clustering (Sci. Reports 2024); smurf/gather-scatter detectors (SMoTeF uses temporal order + flow analysis; GARG-AML gives a scalable interpretable score); and peeling-chain heuristics that identify change addresses across peel/propagate/aggregate stages. Rule+learning hybrids use typology templates (smurfing, gather-scatter/scatter-gather, peeling, mixer) as inductive priors or candidate generators, then rank/refine with GNNs.
- **graph_task_type**: Primarily subgraph/motif classification and dense-subgraph (flow-path) detection; also node classification (suspicious accounts) and edge/transaction flagging depending on sub-method
- **temporal_modeling**: Central to this line. LAS-GNN encodes edge timestamps directly in directed message passing and uses an order-sensitive LSTM aggregator to capture the temporal sequence of transactions (funds must move A->B->C in time order to constitute laundering). Temporal-motif mining counts time-ordered 3-transaction sequences within a delta-time window. SMoTeF explicitly reasons over temporal order + flow. CubeFlow adds a time mode to its tensor. FlowScope itself is largely time-agnostic (flow conservation) but is often applied over time-sliced snapshots.
- **supervision_regime**: Mixed: FlowScope/CubeFlow and temporal-motif mining are unsupervised; SMoTeF/GARG-AML/peeling-chain are rule/heuristic (unsupervised or weakly-supervised); LAS-GNN is supervised/semi-supervised on labeled motifs
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Strong. This line explicitly exploits directed multigraph structure, edge amounts, and edge timestamps: FlowScope requires directed flows and transfer amounts; LAS-GNN uses directed timestamp-aware message passing over weighted temporal edges; peeling-chain detection follows directed change-address edges. Directionality and amount conservation are core signals, not optional features.
- **typology_coverage**: Explicit and broad: smurfing (fan-out/structuring), gather-scatter and scatter-gather (mixing viewed as gather-scatter), peeling chains (peel/propagate/aggregate), layering via chains of intermediary accounts, and mixer/tumbler flows. This is the strongest line for mapping named AML typologies to detectable graph structures.
- **chain_coverage**: Bitcoin (peeling chains, temporal-motif crypto studies), general bank/transaction networks (FlowScope, CubeFlow, SMoTeF on injected + real bank data), NFT networks (Sci. Reports 2024), and synthetic AMLworld/AMLSim-style data; multi-chain applicability by analogy

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: IBM AMLworld / AMLSim synthetic laundering data (LAS-GNN, Egressy et al. line); real bank transfer datasets + injected laundering (FlowScope, CubeFlow); two Bitcoin datasets + one NFT dataset (Sci. Reports 2024 temporal motifs); bank/mule datasets (SMoTeF, GARG-AML); real Bitcoin blockchain for peeling-chain forensics; Elliptic/Elliptic2 used by adjacent subgraph-learning work

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Inherently explainable / evidence-native. Detections ARE the explanation: a flagged flow path, a matched motif, or a peeling chain is a human-readable narrative of how funds moved (source -> smurfs -> aggregation). FlowScope returns the specific accounts and path; temporal motifs return the exact time-ordered transaction sequence; peeling-chain detection returns the chain of change addresses. Typologies map directly to named, auditable evidence.
- **explainability_type**: rule-based / motif-native (structural evidence); LAS-GNN adds attention/aggregation-based motif localization

### practicality

- **data_availability**: Bitcoin data is public (blockchain); Elliptic/Elliptic2 public with license; AMLworld/AMLSim synthetic data publicly available; real bank datasets in FlowScope/SMoTeF are typically proprietary/not downloadable
- **llm_integration**: none (classical graph/statistical methods; LLMs could optionally narrate matched motifs but not part of the methods)
- **deployment_maturity**: Mixed: FlowScope/CubeFlow are open-source libraries (spartan2) with production-style guarantees; peeling-chain heuristics are used in commercial forensics tooling (Chainalysis/Merkle Science style); LAS-GNN and temporal-motif studies are academic prototypes
- **integration_difficulty**: Low-to-medium for ChainLens. FlowScope-style flow scoring and peeling-chain / smurfing motif templates map directly onto ChainLens's rule-motif layer and require no labels; they run on the directed, amount/time-weighted transaction graph ChainLens already builds. ### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: This is the most directly adoptable line for ChainLens's rule-motif + evidence-narrative layer. ChainLens can (a) implement smurfing/gather-scatter/scatter-gather and peeling-chain templates as deterministic motif detectors over its directed TRC-20/Elliptic transaction graph, producing named-typology evidence for the narrative output; (b) add a FlowScope/CubeFlow-style flow-conservation anomalousness score to catch layering that pure node-level GCN/GraphSAGE misses; (c) optionally upgrade to LAS-GNN-style timestamp-aware directed message passing with an LSTM aggregator to learn temporal motifs beyond hand-written rules, giving a rule+learning hybrid where rules generate candidates and the GNN ranks them. ## Scalability, federation and privacy in AML graph ML (BlazingAML high-throughput graph mining; Towards Collaborative AML Among Financial Institutions; FHE privacy-preserving graph ML)

### basic_info

- **name**: Scalability, federation and privacy in AML graph ML (BlazingAML high-throughput graph mining; Towards Collaborative AML Among Financial Institutions; FHE privacy-preserving graph ML)
- **item_type**: method
- **year**: 2024-2026 (FHE/Effendi & Chattopadhyay: arXiv 2411.02926, Nov 2024 / SPACE 2024; Collaborative AML/Tian et al.: arXiv 2502.19952, WWW 2025; BlazingAML/Ye et al.: arXiv 2604.12241, Apr 2026)
- **authors_or_org**: BlazingAML: Haojie Ye, Arjun Laxman, Yichao Yuan, Krisztian Flautner, Nishil Talati (University of Michigan; cs.DC). Collaborative AML: Zhihua Tian, Yuan Ding, Wenjie Qu, Xiang Yu, Enchao Gong, Jian Liu, Kui Ren (Zhejiang University / Ant Group-Alipay; WWW 2025). FHE AML: Fabrianne Effendi, Anupam Chattopadhyay (Nanyang Technological University; SPACE 2024).

### method

- **core_method**: Three complementary works on the PRODUCTION and CROSS-INSTITUTION constraints of AML graph ML. (1) BlazingAML (systems/throughput) - a scalable AML system built around a MULTI-STAGE abstraction that decomposes complex laundering schemes into logical stages connected by graph operations, expressing fuzzy (structurally and temporally variable) patterns with unified primitives instead of enumerating every variant; a DOMAIN-SPECIFIC COMPILER lowers high-level pattern descriptions to optimized parallel code for CPU and GPU back-ends, removing the need for analysts to hand-write parallel code. Focus is execution efficiency of graph pattern mining, not a new learning model. (2) Towards Collaborative AML Among Financial Institutions (federation) - the FIRST algorithm enabling AML detection across MULTIPLE institutions while protecting local data privacy/security; combines federated learning with graph-based methods so institutions collaboratively detect cross-institution laundering subgroups without centralizing their transaction graphs. (3) Privacy-Preserving Graph ML with FHE (privacy/crypto) - runs AML inference directly on ENCRYPTED data using Fully Homomorphic Encryption over the Torus (TFHE) via Zama Concrete ML; two pipelines: (a) an FHE-compatible GNN made deployable via quantization and pruning, and (b) a graph-based XGBoost pipeline using IBM's Graph Feature Preprocessor (GFP) to precompute graph features fed to an encrypted-inference tree model.
- **graph_task_type**: BlazingAML: subgraph/pattern (motif) mining/matching over the transaction graph. Collaborative AML: node/subgraph-level illicit-account and cross-institution laundering-subgroup detection. FHE: node-level illicit-transaction/account classification (GNN embeddings + XGBoost on graph features).
- **supervision_regime**: BlazingAML: rule/pattern-driven graph mining (analyst-specified patterns; unsupervised-style matching rather than trained labels for the mining stage). Collaborative AML: supervised federated graph learning on labeled institutional data. FHE: supervised (GNN + XGBoost trained on labeled AML datasets).
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Yes across all three - directed money-transfer graphs with edge attributes (amount, timestamp) are central. BlazingAML's stages are connected by directed graph operations that exploit direction and edge attributes; Collaborative AML models cross-institution directed transfers; the FHE GFP pipeline derives directed graph features (fan-in/out, temporal amount aggregates) for each node.
- **typology_coverage**: BlazingAML: broad, user-expressible fuzzy multi-stage schemes (layering across multiple hops/stages; structuring; variants captured by the fuzzy abstraction) - the whole point is expressing many typologies with unified primitives. Collaborative AML: CROSS-INSTITUTION laundering where the launderer splits activity across banks (the typology that centralized detectors miss). FHE: generic AML classification, typology-agnostic (whatever the labeled dataset encodes).

### practicality

- **llm_integration**: none (all three are classical graph-ML / systems / crypto works; no LLM component).
- **integration_difficulty**: BlazingAML: medium-high - adopting its multi-stage pattern DSL + compiler is a substantial engineering lift, but the payoff (210-333x) is high for ChainLens's rule-motif layer; conceptually it is a faster engine for exactly ChainLens's hand-coded motif rules. Collaborative AML: high - federated cross-institution setup requires partner institutions and infrastructure ChainLens does not have as a single analytics tool; more a future direction than a near-term integration. FHE: high - TFHE/Concrete ML integration is heavy and slow; only worth it if ChainLens needs encrypted cross-org collaboration.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: These three define the PRODUCTION-SCALE and CROSS-INSTITUTION constraints ChainLens will eventually hit, and each maps to a concrete ChainLens component. BlazingAML is the most directly actionable: ChainLens already uses rule motifs (smurfing, gather-scatter, peel chains) - BlazingAML shows how to express such patterns as fuzzy multi-stage specs and COMPILE them to CPU/GPU code for 210-333x throughput, which matters because ChainLens targets CPU feasibility and real-time on TRON/USDT graphs; even without adopting the compiler, the multi-stage fuzzy-pattern abstraction is a better design for ChainLens's motif engine than enumerating variants. The Collaborative AML paper is the reference for the cross-institution/cross-VASP laundering typology that single-graph detectors (including ChainLens on one chain) structurally miss, and shows federated graph learning at 200M-account scale is viable - a roadmap item if ChainLens ever federates across data providers. ## Temporal GNNs for transaction graphs (EvolveGCN, TGAT/TGN, MDST-GNN, ATGAT / Temporal-Aware Graph Attention, ChronoWave-GNN / Wavelet-Temporal Graph Transformer)

### basic_info

- **name**: Temporal GNNs for transaction graphs (EvolveGCN, TGAT/TGN, MDST-GNN, ATGAT / Temporal-Aware Graph Attention, ChronoWave-GNN / Wavelet-Temporal Graph Transformer)
- **item_type**: trend
- **year**: 2020-2026 (EvolveGCN AAAI 2020; TGAT ICLR 2020; TGN ICML-W 2020; MDST-GNN 2025; ATGAT arXiv 2506.21382 Jun 2025; ChronoWave-GNN Scientific Reports 2025/2026)
- **authors_or_org**: EvolveGCN: Pareja et al., IBM Research/MIT-IBM Watson. TGAT: da Xu et al., Walmart Labs. TGN: Rossi et al., Twitter/Emanuele Rossi. MDST-GNN: Chen et al. (Advanced Intelligent Systems, Wiley, 2025). ATGAT: Zhi Zheng, Bochuan Zhou, Yuping Song, Shanghai Normal University. ChronoWave-GNN: Scientific Reports (Nature) authors, 2025/2026.

### method

- **core_method**: A family of models that add explicit temporal dynamics to GNNs on transaction graphs. (1) EvolveGCN: instead of node embeddings, it evolves the GCN weight matrices over time with an RNN/GRU (or LSTM), so the model adapts to concept drift without requiring nodes to persist across snapshots - a discrete-time snapshot approach. (2) TGAT (Temporal Graph Attention): continuous-time; encodes elapsed time with a functional/Bochner time-encoding and uses self-attention over temporal neighbourhoods to produce time-aware node embeddings, enabling inductive prediction on unseen nodes/timestamps. (3) TGN (Temporal Graph Networks): continuous-time event stream model with a per-node MEMORY module updated by each interaction (via GRU) plus a graph-attention embedding module; generalises TGAT/DySAT and handles edge/event streams. (4) MDST-GNN: multi-distance spatial-temporal GNN that combines multi-distance graph convolution (local + global spatial dependencies) with adaptive temporal modelling and self-supervised learning, plus an attention mechanism for interpretability. (5) ATGAT (Augmented Temporal-aware Graph Attention): fuses multi-scale time-difference features with periodic positional encoding, adds a temporal-aware triple-attention mechanism (structural + temporal + global context) and weighted BCE for imbalance. (6) ChronoWave-GNN (Wavelet-Temporal Graph Transformer): integrates discrete wavelet transforms with temporal-aware attention to jointly model multiscale structural patterns and irregular transaction dynamics.
- **graph_task_type**: node classification (Elliptic-style transaction/node illicit-vs-licit); several models (TGN/TGAT) also do edge/link prediction and dynamic-edge anomaly scoring
- **temporal_modeling**: This is the defining axis. Two paradigms: (a) DISCRETE-TIME snapshot models - EvolveGCN, DySAT, MDST-GNN, Balanced-BiEGCN - process a sequence of graph snapshots (Elliptic has 49 time steps) and use RNN/attention across snapshots; EvolveGCN specifically evolves GCN parameters over time. (b) CONTINUOUS-TIME event models - TGAT (functional time encoding), TGN (node memory + time encoding over the event stream) - model exact inter-event time gaps. ATGAT adds multi-scale time-difference + periodic position encodings; ChronoWave-GNN adds wavelet (frequency-domain) decomposition of temporal signals. Temporal modeling is what lets these models respect the Elliptic temporal train/val/test split (first ~70% steps train, next ~15% val, final ~15% test) and cope with concept drift / distribution shift over time.
- **class_imbalance_handling**: Handled mostly via loss weighting and sampling: weighted / focal / weighted-BCE loss (ATGAT uses weighted BCE), class-balanced learning networks (Balanced-BiEGCN adds an explicit class-balanced module), and minority-focused evaluation (illicit-F1, PR-AUC). MDST-GNN leans on self-supervised pretraining to improve minority generalisation. Temporal splits worsen imbalance drift (e.g. the Elliptic 'dark market shutdown' after step 43 sharply degrades illicit recall), which several papers flag.
- **supervision_regime**: predominantly supervised (Elliptic labels); MDST-GNN adds self-supervised pretraining; TGAT/TGN are inductive and support self-supervised link-prediction pretraining then supervised fine-tuning
- **edge_and_direction_support**: Variable. TGN/TGAT natively consume timestamped edge/event streams and edge features (amount, time). EvolveGCN and Elliptic-based snapshot models often use the provided node feature matrix and treat the graph as (largely) undirected. Directionality of transfers is generally under-exploited in this family - a gap versus directed-multigraph methods like Multi-GNN. Edge features (amount, time-delta) are increasingly used (ATGAT time-diff encoding).
- **chain_coverage**: Primarily Bitcoin (Elliptic and Elliptic++ transaction graphs). ATGAT uses Elliptic++; MDST-GNN and ChronoWave-GNN use Elliptic. Methods are chain-agnostic and transferable to Ethereum/TRON transfer streams.

### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: Elliptic (203,769 Bitcoin transactions, 234,355 edges, 166 features incl. 1 time-step + 165 tx features, 49 time steps, ~4,545 illicit / ~21% labelled). Elliptic++ (adds actor/address-level graph and more labels; used by ATGAT). Some works add custom exchange/phishing datasets (e.g. TGAT for phishing).
- **key_metrics**: On Elliptic, static GCN reaches ~0.95 AUROC / illicit-F1 ~0.7-0.8; GAT ~0.96; adding temporal modeling pushes AUROC past ~0.97. ATGAT on Elliptic++ reports AUC 0.9130 (+9.2% over XGBoost, +12.0% over GCN, +10.0% over GAT). MDST-GNN reports +1.5% AUC-ROC and +2.9% AUC-PR over prior SOTA on Elliptic. ChronoWave-GNN (wavelet-temporal) reports test accuracy 0.9802 and F1 0.9799 on Elliptic, beating GraphSAGE/GAT/TGAT/DySAT/GraphMLP. CAUTION: recent critical re-evaluations (arXiv 2604.19514) show much of the apparent temporal-GNN advantage collapses under strict inductive/temporal-shift protocols - the post-step-43 illicit drop is the standard failure mode, so headline F1s are often optimistic.

### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: Mostly attention-native: attention weights over temporal neighbours (TGAT/TGN/ATGAT) and node/edge importance (MDST-GNN explicitly surfaces the most influential nodes and edges via its attention mechanism). No standard post-hoc explainer (GNNExplainer/SubgraphX) is default; explanations are attention-heatmap style rather than narrative or motif-labelled.
- **explainability_type**: attention-native (self-attention weights over temporal neighbourhoods); no built-in SHAP/GNNExplainer post-hoc layer

### practicality

- **data_availability**: Strong. Elliptic dataset is public on Kaggle (research license); Elliptic++ is public on GitHub. This makes reproduction and ChainLens benchmarking straightforward.
- **scalability**: Elliptic-scale (~204k nodes, ~234k edges, 49 snapshots) is small by GNN standards and is comfortably trainable on CPU or a single modest GPU. Discrete-snapshot models (EvolveGCN, MDST-GNN) are the most CPU-friendly and fastest to train. Continuous-time TGN is heavier due to the per-node memory module and sequential event processing (harder to parallelise, larger memory), so it is the least CPU-friendly and hardest to scale to millions of streaming edges; TGAT is intermediate. For CPU-only ChainLens, EvolveGCN / lightweight temporal attention are the practical picks; full TGN real-time streaming needs care.
- **llm_integration**: none (DynBERG is a related BERT-based dynamic variant, but the core temporal-GNN family here has no LLM component)
- **deployment_maturity**: EvolveGCN/TGAT/TGN are mature open-source libraries integrated into PyG / PyG-Temporal (production-adjacent). MDST-GNN, ATGAT, ChronoWave-GNN are academic prototypes (recent journal/preprint results, limited released code).
- **integration_difficulty**: Low-to-medium. EvolveGCN and TGN/TGAT are available in PyTorch Geometric (Temporal), so bolting a temporal module onto ChainLens's existing GCN/GraphSAGE pipeline on Elliptic is low effort and respects the temporal split ChainLens already cares about. Medium effort to (a) build timestamped edge streams for TRON TRC-20, and (b) reproduce MDST-GNN/ATGAT/ChronoWave-GNN without official code. TGN's memory module adds engineering and CPU-cost complexity for real-time use.

### chainlens_relevance

- **relevance_to_chainlens**: Directly relevant to ChainLens's Elliptic temporal evaluation and future TRON streaming. ChainLens uses Elliptic with a temporal split; temporal GNNs are the natural upgrade over static GCN/GraphSAGE because they model concept drift across the 49 time steps and typically add a few points of illicit-F1/AUROC. For CPU feasibility, EvolveGCN (evolving weights, no persistent node memory) and lightweight temporal-attention variants (ATGAT-style time-difference encoding + weighted BCE) are the best fit; full TGN is likely too heavy for CPU/real-time. The attention-native importance from MDST-GNN/ATGAT can feed ChainLens's evidence-narrative layer (which nodes/edges/time-steps drove the alert). Critically, ChainLens should adopt the strict inductive temporal protocol and report illicit-F1/PR-AUC (not accuracy/AUROC alone), because recent re-evaluations show temporal-GNN gains partly evaporate under distribution shift after Elliptic step 43 - a realistic caution for production claims.
- **key_takeaways**: Start with EvolveGCN (or a light temporal-attention model) on ChainLens's existing Elliptic pipeline - discrete-snapshot temporal models are CPU-friendly, PyG-Temporal-ready, and model the 49-step concept drift the static GCN ignores.<br>Temporal modeling reliably adds a few points on Elliptic (AUROC 0.95->~0.97+; ATGAT AUC 0.9130 on Elliptic++; ChronoWave-GNN F1 ~0.98 on Elliptic) - but treat headline numbers skeptically and validate under a strict inductive temporal split with illicit-F1 / PR-AUC.<br>Continuous-time TGN/TGAT give the richest edge-stream modeling (native timestamped transfers, good for future TRON real-time), but TGN's per-node memory is the heaviest for CPU/streaming - budget accordingly.<br>Use the attention weights (MDST-GNN, ATGAT) as ready-made node/edge/time-step attributions to enrich ChainLens's evidence-narrative output; no extra post-hoc explainer needed.<br>Be aware of the gap vs directed-multigraph methods: most temporal-GNN work under-uses edge direction and does not enumerate laundering typologies - combining a temporal module with Multi-GNN-style reverse-MP/ports/edge-MLPs is the strongest direction for ChainLens.

## TRON USDT stablecoin fraud flow studies

### basic_info

- **name**: TRON USDT stablecoin fraud flow studies
- **item_type**: trend
- **year**: 2024-2026
- **authors_or_org**: John M. Griffin & Kevin Mei (UT Austin, SSRN 2024/rev.2025); Luciano Juvinski, Haochen Li, Alessio Brini (StableAML, arXiv 2602.17842, 2026); Tether / TRON / TRM Labs (T3 Financial Crime Unit); BlockSec / crypto.news (USDT blacklisting analytics)

### method

- **core_method**: A cluster of empirical / forensic-finance and applied-AML work characterizing illicit stablecoin flows (predominantly USDT on TRON and Ethereum). (1) Griffin & Mei, 'How Do Crypto Flows Finance Slavery? The Economics of Pig Butchering' (SSRN 4742235): forensic on-chain tracing — collected crypto addresses from 4,000+ pig-butchering victims and traced fund flows from victims through scammer wallets to exchange deposit accounts, quantifying volumes, fees, and the central role of Tether. (2) StableAML (arXiv 2602.17842): builds a large stablecoin-specific labeled dataset (USDT+USDC full transfer history on Ethereum) from Etherscan reports, security firms and the OFAC SDN list, with 68 domain-specific behavioral wallet features across four categories (Interaction, Derived Network, Transfer-based, Temporal/Direct), and benchmarks tree-ensemble models vs GNNs for wallet-level illicit detection. (3) T3 Financial Crime Unit: operational public-private tracing/freezing pipeline combining TRM Labs analytics with Tether's USDT freeze authority on TRON. (4) USDT blacklisting practice studies (BlockSec/crypto.news): empirical measurement of Tether's on-chain blacklist (freeze) events as ground-truth illicit labels.
- **graph_task_type**: Mixed: address/wallet-level node classification (StableAML behavioral wallet detection); flow-tracing / path analysis rather than formal graph-ML in Griffin & Mei and the T3/blacklisting work
- **temporal_modeling**: Griffin & Mei track flows over calendar time (2020-2024) to estimate annual volumes and observe the growth of pig-butchering deposits. StableAML includes explicit Temporal and Direct features (velocity, timing of transfers) and finds cybercrime-syndicate wallets exhibit high-velocity dispersion vs the static footprints of sanctioned entities. Blacklisting studies are time-stamped freeze-event series (e.g. $515M frozen across 371 wallets in a 30-day window). No continuous-time GNN modeling.

- **typology_coverage**: Strong ground-truth coverage of real-world laundering/fraud typologies on TRON/USDT: pig-butchering (romance/investment scams financing forced-labor 'scam compounds' in SE Asia), gather-scatter / dispersion patterns (StableAML high-velocity syndicate flows), sanctions-evasion and terrorism financing (OFAC SDN wallets, ISIS-K-linked TRON wallets, North Korea-linked flows), and exchange cash-out (funds consolidated into suspicious exchange deposit accounts). ### evaluation

- **datasets_used**: Griffin & Mei: proprietary set of crypto addresses from 4,000+ pig-butchering victims + on-chain flow tracing (2020-2024). ### explainability

- **explainability_approach**: StableAML emphasizes interpretability: its domain-feature tree ensembles let analysts read WHY a wallet is flagged and successfully DIFFERENTIATE high-velocity cybercrime-syndicate dispersion from the static footprints of OFAC-sanctioned entities.
